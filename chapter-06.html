Chapter 6 · Foundation

Telecommunications and Networks

How computers talk to each other: the internet, private company networks, the kinds of networks, and the cables and airwaves that carry the signals.

Good to know: mostly a background chapter for this course. The one part that genuinely matters elsewhere is the difference between the internet, an intranet, and an extranet, so make sure that one sticks. The rest is for comfortable vocabulary.
In plain English - the whole chapter as one story

Our running example: BrewHaus, now with shops in several cities. Those shops, the head office, the suppliers and the customers all need to exchange information. This chapter is about the plumbing that connects them: networks.

1. A network is just things connected together

Connect two computers and you have a tiny network. The striking thing is that a network gets much more valuable as more people join, far faster than you would expect. That is why the big networks (like the internet) became unstoppable.

2. Three flavours of network, told apart by who is allowed in

The internet is open to everyone. An intranet is a private version just for BrewHaus staff. An extranet opens a slice of that private network to specific outsiders, like your bean supplier. Same technology, different doors.

3. Networks knock down barriers

They let BrewHaus beat distance (a head-office report reaches every city instantly), time, awkward structures, and cost. That is the business reason to invest in them.

4. Every network is built from the same handful of parts

Devices at each end, processors that direct traffic, channels that carry the signal, computers, and software to run it. Once you can name those, any network diagram makes sense.

5. Networks come in sizes and shapes

Small ones inside a building (LAN), big ones spanning cities (WAN), secure tunnels across the public internet (VPN), and different ways to share the work (client/server or peer-to-peer).

How the chapter connects: a network is connected things that grow more valuable with size → the internet, intranet and extranet differ only by who can access them → networks create value by removing barriers of distance, time, structure and cost → each network is built from devices, processors, channels, computers and software → and they come in different sizes (LAN, WAN, VPN) and work-sharing styles (client/server, peer-to-peer), carried over cables and airwaves.

Section 1

What a network is, and why size matters so much

Why is a network with many users worth so much more than one with few?

A network is simply an interconnected group of things, here, computers and devices, each connection point being a node. The number of possible connections grows fast: with N nodes there are N(N-1) possible links. Ten computers already allow ninety.

Metcalfe's law. The usefulness of a network grows roughly with the square of the number of users. Double the users and you roughly quadruple the value. There is a tipping point called critical mass: below it, a new technology only affects itself; above it, whole social and economic systems shift. This is exactly why the internet kept getting more valuable the more people joined, and why a loyalty network is worth far more at 50,000 members than at 500.
Section 2 · the part that matters

Internet, intranet, extranet

These three words sound alike. What is the actual difference?

They all use the same internet technology (browsers, web pages, the standard internet protocols). The only difference is who is allowed in:

  • Internet - the public, global network. No owner, no central computer, open to all. BrewHaus uses it to reach customers with its website and online store.
  • Intranet - a private internal network, built with internet technology but walled off for employees only, protected by passwords, encryption and firewalls. BrewHaus's staff handbook, rotas and internal dashboards live here.
  • Extranet - extends part of that intranet outward to specific trusted partners over a secure link. BrewHaus lets its bean supplier see stock levels and reorder, but nothing else.
Memory hook: intranet = inside only; extranet = inside shared with chosen outsiders; internet = open to all.
Section 3

Why networks are valuable to a business

Beyond "computers can chat," what do networks actually buy you?

They give a business strategic reach by knocking down four barriers:

🌍

Geographic

Connect distant shops and offices as if they were in one room.

⏱️

Time

Share information instantly instead of waiting for post or travel.

🏢

Structural

Let separate departments and partners work together smoothly.

💵

Cost

Do business more cheaply by moving information instead of people and paper.

On top of that, the internet itself opens new revenue sources, new web-based products, new markets and channels, and stronger customer loyalty.

Section 4

The parts of any network

If I drew any network, what pieces would always be in the picture?

Five components make up every telecommunications network:

ComponentWhat it is
Terminals / devicesThe PCs, tills and phones that send and receive.
Telecommunications processorsThe traffic directors: modems, switches, routers, hubs.
ChannelsThe media that carry the signal: cables or airwaves.
ComputersThe host or server at the other end.
SoftwareThe programs that manage and run the network.

A simple way to hold it: a sender transmits a message to a receiver over a channel made of some medium.

Section 5

Types of networks

Are all networks the same shape and size?

No. A few distinctions are worth knowing:

  • LAN (local area network) - covers a small area like one shop or office, to share local resources (a printer, a server, files). One BrewHaus branch has a LAN.
  • WAN (wide area network) - spans a large geographic area, tying distant sites together. BrewHaus's cities are linked by a WAN.
  • VPN (virtual private network) - a secure, private "tunnel" across the public internet, so two sites can communicate privately without paying for dedicated lines.

There are also two ways to divide the work across a network:

Client/serverPeer-to-peer (P2P)
Work is split: clients (the user-facing front end) request, and servers (the shared back end) provide databases, applications and heavy processing. Computers connect directly as equals and share files with each other, with no permanent boss.

Peer-to-peer comes in two forms: a central-server version where a central directory lists who has what (more secure, but the directory can become a bottleneck), and a pure version with no central server at all (more resilient, but harder to secure and control).

Section 6

The cables and the airwaves

What physically carries the signal?

Two families: things you run a wire through, and things you send through the air.

Wired media, from slowest to fastest:

  • Twisted-pair wire - ordinary copper phone wire.
  • Coaxial cable - thicker, shielded copper, like cable TV.
  • Fiber-optic cable - hair-thin glass carrying pulses of light: huge capacity, very fast, immune to electrical interference.

Wireless media: terrestrial microwave (line-of-sight relays), communications satellites (great for remote areas, but a slight delay makes them poor for real-time interaction), cellular phone systems, Wi-Fi wireless LANs, and short-range Bluetooth for nearby devices.

The "last mile" problem. Providers run super-fast fibre as a backbone, but the final stretch into many homes is still old twisted-pair wire. The slow last link cancels out the fast backbone, like a motorway that ends in a dirt track at your driveway.

One more pair worth knowing: an analog signal is a continuous wave (old voice phone); a digital signal is discrete numbers. Digital is faster, cheaper, lower-error, and can carry data, voice and video on the same line, which is why everything moved to digital.

Section 7

How devices agree to talk

How do machines from different makers manage to understand each other?

By agreeing on protocols: standard rules for how to communicate. The internet's rulebook is TCP/IP. There is also a teaching model called the OSI model that breaks communication into seven layers (from the physical wires at the bottom up to the application you use at the top), each layer handling one part of the job. You do not need the seven names by heart for this course; just know networking is organised in layers, and TCP/IP is the standard that makes the internet work.

Two final ideas you may meet:

  • Circuit switching vs packet switching. Circuit switching opens one dedicated line for the whole conversation (a traditional phone call). Packet switching chops the message into small packets that travel separately and reassemble at the end (how the internet works) - far more efficient.
  • VoIP (voice over IP) - making phone calls over the internet instead of the phone network, skipping long-distance charges. Skype is the classic example.
