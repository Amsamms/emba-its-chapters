Chapter 11 · Exam favourite

Security and Ethical Challenges

The dark side of technology: the ethical dilemmas it raises, the crimes it enables, the threats to privacy, and the defenses a business uses to protect itself.

Exam radar: heavily examined and directly tied to Egypt's two famous laws. Lock in the six ethical dimensions, the main types of computer crime, and the key security defenses (especially encryption and firewalls). All flagged below.
In plain English - the whole chapter as one story

Our running example: BrewHaus now holds thousands of customer records and runs everything on connected systems. That power brings risk and responsibility. This chapter is about the trouble technology can cause, and how to guard against it.

1. Technology cuts both ways

It does great good and real harm. A professional's job is to push the benefits up and the harms down. The harms cluster into six areas, from jobs to privacy to health.

2. Ethics needs a compass

"Is this right?" is answered with theories (who do you owe a duty to?) and principles (does the good outweigh the harm? did people consent?). These give you a way to reason, not just a gut feeling.

3. Crime moved online

Hacking, theft, viruses and scams all threaten BrewHaus's systems. Knowing the named attacks helps you recognise and defend against them.

4. Privacy is the big battleground

Every click leaves data. The core question is consent: must you opt in before a company collects your data, or are you tracked unless you opt out? Different places answer differently.

5. Defense is layered

No single wall keeps everyone out. BrewHaus combines encryption, firewalls, passwords, backups and disaster plans, each covering what the others miss.

How the chapter connects: technology brings benefit and harm → the harm falls into six ethical dimensions → ethical theories and principles help you judge what is right → online crime (hacking, malware, theft) is the concrete threat → privacy is the central concern, hinging on consent → and a layered set of security defenses protects the business. All of this is the global backdrop to Egypt's own data-protection and cyber-crime laws.

Section 1 · exam favourite

The six ethical dimensions of IT

In how many ways can technology cause ethical trouble?

The chapter groups the ethical and social challenges of technology into six dimensions. Memorise them, they are a common exam list:

💼

Employment

New jobs and productivity, but also lost jobs and the need for new skills.

🔒

Privacy

The power to collect and store data threatens personal privacy.

🚨

Crime

Technology enables new kinds of crime and abuse.

🏭

Working conditions

It removes dull tasks but can create repetitive, monotonous ones.

🧍

Individuality

Rigid systems can depersonalise and dehumanise work.

🩺

Health

Repetitive computer work causes ailments like carpal tunnel syndrome.

The guiding idea for a professional: manage your work to minimise the harm and maximise the benefit across all six.

Section 2

Ethics theories and principles

When you ask "is this the right thing to do?", how do you actually decide?

Two toolkits help. First, three theories of responsibility, which answer "who do I owe a duty to?":

TheoryWho the manager owes a duty to
Stockholder theoryOnly the shareholders: maximise profit, within the law and without fraud.
Social contract theoryAll of society.
Stakeholder theoryAll stakeholders: customers, employees, suppliers, community, not just shareholders.

Second, four principles of technology ethics for deciding whether to deploy a technology:

  • Proportionality - the good must outweigh the harm, with no less-harmful alternative.
  • Informed consent - those affected understand and accept the risks.
  • Justice - benefits and burdens are shared fairly.
  • Minimised risk - even if acceptable, avoid all unnecessary risk.

And a practical reminder: a company must set ethical policies and make sure employees know them, otherwise it cannot hold anyone accountable.

Section 3 · exam favourite

Computer crime

What counts as computer crime, and what are the common attacks?

Computer crime covers the unauthorised use, access, modification or destruction of hardware, software, data or networks, plus illegal copying of software and denying users access to their own resources. Some vocabulary first:

  • Hacking - unauthorised access to or use of networked systems.
  • Cracker - a malicious or criminal hacker.
  • Breaking and entering - getting in and reading files without stealing or damaging.

The common hacking tactics are worth recognising:

TacticWhat it does
Denial of service (DoS)Floods a site with requests until it slows or crashes.
ScansProbes the internet to find weaknesses to exploit.
SnifferSecretly reads data packets to capture passwords or content.
SpoofingFakes an email or web page to trick users into giving up secrets.
Trojan horseA program hiding malicious instructions inside something useful.
Back doorA hidden way back in, left for easy re-entry.
Social engineeringTricking employees into revealing passwords or information.
Dumpster divingSifting the rubbish for useful information.

Related threats include cyber theft (often an inside job to steal money), cyberterrorism (using systems to cause real-world harm), unauthorised use at work (theft of time and resources), and software piracy (illegal copying, which loses the software industry roughly a third of its revenue).

Section 4

Viruses, worms and spyware

What is the difference between all these nasties?

Virus versus worm (a favourite exam distinction). A virus cannot run on its own, it has to insert itself into another (host) program. A worm is a standalone program that can run and spread by itself. Both copy themselves to other computers, usually via email attachments, the internet, infected disks or shareware.

Two more to keep straight: adware shows you ads (sometimes with a useful purpose), while spyware is adware that secretly sends information about you back over the internet without your knowledge. Spyware is the sneaky one.

Section 5 · exam favourite

Privacy

Every click leaves a trail. Who gets to decide what is collected about you?

The power of technology to store and retrieve information threatens privacy: personal data is collected on every website visit, and confidential records get stolen or misused. The central question is consent, and the world answers it two ways:

Opt-inOpt-out
Data can be collected only if you explicitly agree first. The default in Europe. (More protective of the individual.) Data can be collected unless you specifically ask them to stop. The default in the US. (More permissive for business.)

Privacy harms come in several forms: violation of privacy (reading private emails and records), computer monitoring (always knowing where someone is), computer matching (combining data from many sources to profile people), and unauthorised access to personal files. Related issues include spamming (mass unsolicited email) versus flaming (abusive messages), and cyberlaw (the body of law that tries to regulate the internet).

Section 6

Jobs, health and ergonomics

How does technology affect the people who work with it every day?

Beyond crime and privacy, technology reshapes work itself. It can destroy jobs as well as create them and demand new skills (employment). It can make work repetitive and monotonous or use rigid systems that feel impersonal (working conditions and individuality). And it causes real health problems: cumulative trauma disorders from fast repetitive keyboard work, including carpal tunnel syndrome.

The cure on the health side is ergonomics (also called human-factors engineering): designing healthy, comfortable workplaces, the right tools, workstation and tasks around the human operator, which also lifts morale and productivity.

Section 7 · exam favourite

Security defenses

The internet was built for connection, not safety. So how does a business protect itself?

With layered defenses, because no single one is enough. The two the exam cares about most:

Encryption. Data is sent in scrambled form and unscrambled only for authorised users. The common method uses a pair of keys: a public key you share with everyone, and a private key only you hold. To send BrewHaus a secret message, you encrypt it with BrewHaus's public key; only BrewHaus's private key can decrypt it. Even if intercepted, it stays unreadable.
Firewall. A gatekeeper that sits between a company's network and the outside, filtering traffic and acting as a safe transfer point. An external firewall keeps out unauthorised internet users; an internal one stops staff reaching sensitive data they should not see. A firewall deters hacking but cannot fully prevent it.

The rest of the toolkit:

  • Authentication and biometrics - passwords, smart cards, and physical traits (fingerprint, retina, voice).
  • Antivirus software, kept centrally updated.
  • Backup files - duplicate copies of data and programs.
  • Fault-tolerant systems - redundant parts so the system survives a failure, in three modes: fail-over (switch to backups), fail-safe (keep running at the same level), and fail-soft (keep running at a reduced but acceptable level).
  • Disaster recovery plan - formal procedures for who does what, on which systems, after a disaster.
  • Information system controls at every stage (input, processing, output, storage) and regular security audits.
Section 8 · Egypt connection

The Egypt connection (exam tie-in)

How does all this connect to the Egyptian laws the exam keeps asking about?

This chapter is the global backdrop to two Egyptian laws you are often asked to discuss as a citizen:

  • The Personal Data Protection Law (No. 151 of 2020) puts the privacy ideas from this chapter, consent, control over your data, into national law: it regulates how organisations collect, use and protect personal data, and leans toward an opt-in, consent-based approach.
  • The Anti-Cyber and Information Technology Crimes Law (No. 175 of 2018) turns the computer-crime ideas here, hacking, unauthorised access, data theft, into defined offences with penalties.

A common, fair critique to offer: both laws are strong and bring Egypt into line with international practice, but their impact has been weakened by delays in issuing the executive regulations that make them fully operational. (For the full detail of Law 151, see the dedicated study material your course already produced; here, just connect the chapter's privacy and crime themes to these two laws.)

Q1Name the six ethical dimensions of IT.

Q2Give the three theories of social responsibility.
Stockholder theory (duty to shareholders only), social contract theory (duty to all society), and stakeholder theory (duty to all stakeholders).
Q3What is the difference between a virus and a worm?
A virus cannot run without inserting itself into a host program; a worm is a standalone program that runs and spreads on its own.
Q4Opt-in versus opt-out, and where is each the default?
Opt-in means data is collected only with your explicit consent (default in Europe); opt-out means it is collected unless you ask them to stop (default in the US).
Q5How does public/private key encryption work?
You encrypt a message with the recipient's public key, which everyone can have; only the recipient's private key can decrypt it, so an interceptor cannot read it.

A gatekeeper system that filters traffic between a network and the outside (external firewalls block outsiders, internal ones protect sensitive data). It deters hacking but cannot fully prevent it.
