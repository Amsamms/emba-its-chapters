Chapter 7 · Exam favourite

e-Business Systems

The big company-wide systems that join up the whole business: ERP, CRM and SCM, plus the tools that integrate them and the systems that serve each department.

Exam radar: a heavily examined chapter. Lock in the three big initials - ERP (internal backbone), CRM (customers), SCM (suppliers) - what each is, who it faces, and why ERP projects cost and fail so much. All flagged below.
In plain English - the whole chapter as one story

Our running example: BrewHaus has grown into a real company with departments, suppliers and thousands of customers. The problem now: each department has its own systems that do not talk to each other. This chapter is about the big systems that join the whole business up.

1. "e-business" is bigger than "e-commerce"

e-commerce is just the buying and selling online. e-business is the whole thing: selling, plus internal collaboration and running business processes with technology.

2. The useful systems cross department lines

Taking an order touches sales, the kitchen, finance and delivery. A system that only serves one department is not enough; you need cross-functional systems that span them all.

3. Three giant systems, each facing a different direction

ERP is the internal backbone that runs BrewHaus itself. CRM faces outward to customers. SCM faces outward to suppliers. Picture ERP in the centre with CRM and SCM reaching out to either side.

4. CRM keeps the customers

It gives every staff member one complete view of each customer, and works to acquire, grow and keep them. It is far cheaper to keep a customer than to win a new one, which is the whole point.

5. ERP is powerful but brutal to install

It ties every internal process into one system on one database. The catch: most of the cost is not the software, it is redesigning how the company works, and a botched ERP can cripple a business.

6. SCM gets the right stuff to the right place

It coordinates suppliers so BrewHaus has the right beans, in the right shop, at the right time, without drowning in stock. The clever idea: "substitute information for inventory."

How the chapter connects: e-business is the wide umbrella → the systems that matter cross departments → three giants organise the company, ERP in the centre, CRM facing customers, SCM facing suppliers → CRM wins and keeps customers → ERP integrates the insides (at great cost and risk) → SCM coordinates the supply chain → and underneath sit the everyday functional systems for each department.

Section 1 · exam favourite

e-business versus e-commerce

People use these two words as if they mean the same thing. Do they?

No, and the exam likes this distinction. One is a part of the other:

e-commerce is the narrow piece: buying, selling and marketing products, services and information over the internet and other networks.

e-business is the wide umbrella: using the internet and technology to support everything - e-commerce, plus internal communication and collaboration, plus running web-enabled business processes.

So every bit of e-commerce is e-business, but lots of e-business (like BrewHaus's internal staff collaboration) is not e-commerce. e-commerce is a subset of e-business.

Section 2 · exam favourite

Cross-functional systems

Why isn't a separate system for each department good enough?

Because real business processes do not respect department walls. Think about bringing a new BrewHaus seasonal drink to life: it starts in marketing (customer feedback, market research), moves to R&D (recipe design and testing), and ends in operations (production and rollout). One process, three departments.

A cross-functional system is one that deliberately crosses the boundaries of traditional business functions so that an end-to-end process is supported all the way through, instead of being chopped into disconnected pieces. ERP, CRM and SCM are all cross-functional.

Section 4 · exam favourite

ERP: the internal backbone

What exactly is ERP, and why do people fear installing it?

Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) is a cross-functional system that ties all of a company's major internal processes into one integrated suite of software modules sharing one common database, covering production, logistics, accounting and finance, human resources, and sales and order management. Instead of each department keeping its own data, everyone works from the same single source.

ERP benefitsERP challenges
Better quality and efficiency.High risk and high cost.
Lower costs.Hardware and software are only a small part of the project.
Better decision support (one source of truth).A failed rollout can cripple or kill the business.
Greater enterprise agility. 
The exam's favourite ERP fact: most of the money in an ERP project is not the technology. Roughly 43% goes on reengineering the business processes, with training, data conversion and change management eating much of the rest. Hardware and software together are only about a quarter of the cost. ERP is mostly an organisational change project wearing a software costume.

ERP projects fail when companies underestimate the complexity, fail to involve affected employees, over-rely on the vendor, skimp on training and data conversion, or try to do too much, too fast.

Section 5 · exam favourite

CRM: keeping the customer

What does CRM do, and why does it matter so much?

Customer Relationship Management (CRM) is a cross-functional system that integrates and automates the customer-serving processes. Its goal is a single, complete view of every customer for staff, and a single, complete view of the company for the customer. It pulls together five clusters of activity: sales, marketing and fulfillment, customer service and support, retention and loyalty programs, and contact and account management.

Why retention is the goal (memorable numbers). The odds of selling to an existing customer are about 50%, versus only 15% for a new one. It costs around six times more to win a new customer than keep one, an unhappy customer tells 8 to 10 others, and lifting retention by just 5% can lift profits by as much as 85%. That is why BrewHaus's loyalty programme is worth so much.

But CRM is not magic: around 50% of CRM projects fail to deliver what was promised, and about 20% actually damage customer relationships. The usual causes: poor preparation, trying to automate a broken process instead of fixing it first, and not involving the business stakeholders. (Notice the echo of Chapter 5: CRM only works if the underlying customer data is clean.)

Section 6 · exam favourite

SCM: the supply chain

What is supply chain management trying to achieve?

A supply chain is the whole web of suppliers, distributors and partners needed to design, build and sell a product (it is closely related to the value chain from Chapter 2). Supply Chain Management (SCM) coordinates it so the company gets:

The right products, to the right place, at the right time, in the right quantity, at an acceptable cost. Its goals: forecast demand, control inventory, strengthen relationships with suppliers and distributors, and get feedback on the status of every link in the chain.

SCM brings benefits like faster and more accurate order processing, lower inventory, quicker time to market, lower costs, and stronger supplier relationships. Its challenges are weak demand-planning skills, inaccurate data from other systems, poor collaboration between departments, and immature, hard-to-implement tools. An early enabling technology was EDI (electronic data interchange): the electronic exchange of standard business documents (orders, invoices) directly between trading partners.

"Substitute information for inventory." The headline idea of modern SCM: the better and more accurate your information about demand, the less spare stock you need to hold "just in case." Good data lets BrewHaus keep less oat milk sitting around while never running out, money that would otherwise be frozen in a warehouse.
Section 7

Joining it all up: EAI, TPS and collaboration

If a company has all these separate systems, what makes them work together?

  • EAI (enterprise application integration) is the software glue that connects these different systems so they can share data and talk to each other. It acts as middleware linking the customer-facing "front office" (sales, service) with the "back office" (manufacturing, finance), so an order entered once flows automatically to everything that needs it.
  • TPS (transaction processing systems) handle the basic business transactions, sales, purchases, payments. They can run in batch (collect transactions and process them in a group later) or, more commonly today, online / real-time (OLTP), capturing each transaction the instant it happens. BrewHaus's till is a real-time TPS.
  • ECS (enterprise collaboration systems) help teams communicate, coordinate and collaborate, through communication tools (email, messaging), conferencing tools (video, chat) and work management tools (calendars, project and workflow management).
Section 8

The everyday functional systems

Beneath the big three, what serves each individual department?

Each business function has its own information systems. These are the workhorses under the enterprise systems:

FunctionExample systems
MarketingCRM, interactive marketing, sales-force automation, targeted marketing.
Production / operationsManufacturing resource planning, manufacturing execution, process control, computer-integrated manufacturing.
Human resourcesRecruiting, skills inventory, compensation analysis, employee self-service.
AccountingOrder processing, inventory, accounts receivable/payable, payroll, general ledger.
FinanceCash management, credit management, investment, capital budgeting, financial forecasting.

A couple of facts that come up: accounting is the oldest and most widely used business information system, and computer-integrated manufacturing (CIM) has three goals you can remember as Simplify, Automate, Integrate.
