Chapter 8 · Exam favourite

E-Commerce Systems

Selling online: the categories of e-commerce, the processes that make a web store work, how to judge whether a store is any good, the kinds of online marketplace, and mixing online with physical shops.

Exam radar: heavily examined, and one question recurs almost word for word: "how do you evaluate an e-commerce portal, and what are the criteria?" Section 5 is your ready-made answer. Also lock in the three categories and the five marketplace types.
In plain English - the whole chapter as one story

Our running example: BrewHaus decides to sell its coffee beans and merchandise online. This chapter is the playbook for doing e-commerce well.

1. E-commerce is more than a "buy" button

It covers the whole online journey: marketing, selling, taking payment, delivering and supporting. And the seller's steps mirror the buyer's steps the whole way along.

2. It comes in three flavours, by who is on each end

Selling to shoppers is B2C. Selling to other businesses is B2B. Shoppers selling to each other (like an auction site) is C2C.

3. A web store quietly runs nine processes

Behind the friendly storefront, things like security, search, the catalogue, the shopping cart and notifications all have to work. Miss one and the store feels broken.

4. There is a right way to judge a store

You do not rate a web store on looks alone. There is a checklist of success factors, selection, service, look and feel, security, personal attention and more, that tells you if it is actually good.

5. Businesses trade in different "shapes" of marketplace

One seller to many buyers, many sellers to one buyer, many to many like an auction. Five shapes in total, each suiting a different situation.

6. Most winners blend online and physical

Pure-online and pure-physical both have limits. "Clicks and bricks", combining a website with real shops, is what most successful businesses, including a future BrewHaus, end up doing.

How the chapter connects: e-commerce spans the whole online journey → split into B2C, B2B and C2C → powered by nine behind-the-scenes processes → paid for through secure payment → judged by clear success factors → built and run against a requirements checklist → traded in five marketplace shapes → and most successfully delivered by blending clicks with bricks.

Section 1

What e-commerce actually covers

Is e-commerce just the moment money changes hands?

No. E-commerce is the buying, selling, marketing and servicing of products, services and information over networks. It spans the whole online process: selling, marketing, developing, accepting payments, delivering and servicing. And the seller's steps line up against the buyer's steps across three phases, marketing and discovery, transaction processing, then service and support. When BrewHaus markets a coffee subscription, the customer is simultaneously discovering it; when BrewHaus fulfils the order, the customer is receiving it. Two mirrored journeys, one transaction.

Section 2 · exam favourite

The three categories of e-commerce

Who is buying from whom?

E-commerce is grouped by who sits on each end of the deal:

🛍️

B2C

Business to consumer. A business sells to shoppers. Online storefronts, catalogues, customer support. BrewHaus's bean shop is B2C.

🏭

B2B

Business to business. The wholesale and supply side: businesses buy and sell to each other through electronic marketplaces and exchanges.

🔁

C2C

Consumer to consumer. Shoppers sell to other shoppers, like online auctions, classified ads and personal selling sites.

Section 3

The nine essential processes

What is quietly happening behind a working web store?

Every serious e-commerce site runs nine essential processes under the hood. You do not see them, but if any fails, the store feels broken:

ProcessWhat it does
1. Access control & securityEstablish trust: passwords, encryption, certificates; keep people out of restricted areas.
2. Profiling & personalisingLearn about each visitor (registration, cookies) to tailor the experience.
3. Search managementHelp customers find the exact product they want.
4. Content managementCreate, update and deliver the site's text and media.
5. Catalogue managementGenerate and manage the product catalogue, pricing and configuration.
6. PaymentThe shopping cart, payment methods and verification.
7. Workflow managementAutomate the steps of a process (the rules, roles and routing).
8. Event notificationReact to events (an order, a payment, a problem) and tell the right people.
9. Collaboration & tradingSupport negotiation, auctions and online communities.
Section 4

Paying safely online

When a customer types a card number, how is it kept safe?

An online payment bounces between the customer, the merchant and a payment server that connects to the various payment methods (credit cards, bank transfers, PayPal and the like). The merchant verifies the customer and authorises payment; the customer verifies the merchant and confirms the order.

The security risk and the fixes. The danger is that "network sniffers easily recognise credit-card formats", so unprotected card data travelling across the network can be stolen. Three defences: encrypt the data between customer and merchant, encrypt the data between customer and bank, and take the most sensitive information offline altogether.
Section 5 · exam answer

How to judge an e-commerce portal

This is the exam question. So how do you evaluate an online store?

You judge a B2C store against a clear set of success factors. These double as the evaluation criteria for "is this a good portal?":

CriterionThe question it answers
Selection & valueGood range of products at attractive, fair prices?
Performance & serviceFast, reliable, easy to browse, order and receive?
Look and feelAttractive, clear, easy-to-use design?
Advertising & incentivesDoes it attract visitors and reward purchases?
Personal attentionPersonalised pages and one-to-one marketing?
Community relationshipsDoes it build a community (reviews, forums) that creates loyalty?
Security & reliabilityAre payments and data safe, and is the site always up?
CommunicationsGreat customer communication (email, support, updates)?

So to evaluate BrewHaus's online store, you walk this list and score each one. A pretty site that fails on security or service is a bad portal, no matter how it looks.

Section 6

What it takes to run a web store

If BrewHaus wanted to build and run its own store, what is on the to-do list?

A web store has three jobs, each with its own requirements:

JobWhat it requires
Develop the storeBuild it (design tools, templates, hosting) and market it (web ads, email promotions, search-engine registration).
Serve your customersServe (personalised pages, catalogue, shopping cart), transact (card processing, shipping/tax, order emails), and support (online help, customer-service email, chat).
Manage the storeManage (usage and sales reports, account management), operate (24/7 hosting, scalable, redundant servers), and protect (passwords, encryption, firewalls).
Section 7 · exam favourite

The five e-commerce marketplaces

When businesses trade online, what "shapes" can the market take?

There are five marketplace types, named by how many sellers face how many buyers. This is a classic exam list, so learn the pairing of shape, name and meaning:

ShapeNameWhat it means
One to manySell-side marketplaceOne supplier sets the products and prices for many buyers. (A single company's own store.)
Many to oneBuy-side marketplaceMany suppliers bid for the business of one buyer.
Some to manyDistribution marketplaceSeveral suppliers combine their catalogues to attract a bigger audience.
Many to someProcurement marketplaceSeveral big buyers combine their purchasing to attract competition and lower prices.
Many to manyAuction marketplaceMany buyers and sellers, with prices set dynamically by bidding.
Quick memory aid: count the sellers first, then the buyers. One-to-many = sell-side; many-to-one = buy-side; many-to-many = auction.
Section 8 · exam favourite

Clicks and bricks

Should BrewHaus run its website completely separately from its shops, or merge them?

This is the clicks-and-bricks question: how much to integrate the online business with the physical one. It is a genuine trade-off, and there is no single right answer. Think of it as a spectrum:

The further toward separation, the more focus, flexibility and access to outside funding you get (a fully spun-off online company). The further toward integration, the more you reuse the established brand, shared information and existing purchasing and distribution power (an in-house online division). A real cautionary tale: one famous bookseller spun off its website to gain funding and speed, but then failed to win market share, proof that separation has real downsides. The takeaways: there is no universal strategy, both ends have serious pros and cons, and most businesses end up with some blend of clicks-and-bricks integration.

Watch out

Easy to confuse

Thisversus that
B2C - business sells to consumers.B2B - business sells to business; C2C - consumers sell to each other.
Sell-side (one-to-many) - one supplier, many buyers.Buy-side (many-to-one) - many suppliers bidding for one buyer.
Distribution (some-to-many) - suppliers pool catalogues.Procurement (many-to-some) - buyers pool purchasing.
Separation - run the website as an independent business (focus, flexibility, funding).Integration - merge it with the physical business (brand, shared data, leverage).
e-commerce - the online buying and selling.e-business - the wider umbrella (recall Chapter 7).

Q1Name the three categories of e-commerce.
B2C (business to consumer), B2B (business to business), and C2C (consumer to consumer).
Q2How would you evaluate an e-commerce portal? Give the criteria.
Score it on the B2C success factors: selection and value, performance and service, look and feel, advertising and incentives, personal attention, community relationships, security and reliability, and quality of communications.
Q3Name the five e-commerce marketplace types.
Sell-side (one-to-many), buy-side (many-to-one), distribution (some-to-many), procurement (many-to-some), and auction (many-to-many).
Q4What does "clicks and bricks" mean, and what is the key lesson?
Combining online (clicks) with physical (bricks) operations. The lesson: there is no universal strategy, separation and integration both have real pros and cons, and most businesses end up with some blend of integration.
Q5How are online payments kept secure?
Encrypt the data between customer and merchant, encrypt it between customer and bank, and take the most sensitive information offline, because sniffers can recognise card-number formats in transit.
Q6Give three of the nine essential e-commerce processes.
Any three of: access control and security, profiling and personalising, search management, content management, catalogue management, payment, workflow management, event notification, and collaboration and trading.
