Chapter 1 · Foundation

Foundations of Information Systems

The starting point for everything that follows: what an "information system" actually is, what it is made of, and the handful of jobs it does for a business.

In plain English - the whole chapter as one story

Our running example: you own BrewHaus, a small chain of coffee shops. We will follow BrewHaus through all twelve chapters. This first chapter just answers the most basic question: what is an "information system," and why should a coffee shop owner care?

1. First, why does any of this matter?

Information systems are not a nerdy side-topic. They are a vital part of any successful business. They make the work more efficient, help you compete, and help managers make better decisions. If you run BrewHaus blind, with no data, you are guessing. With good systems, you are deciding.

2. What is a "system" in the first place?

A system is just a set of parts that work together toward a shared goal, by taking in something (input), changing it (processing), and putting out a result (output). A coffee machine is a tiny system: beans and water in, hot coffee out. A clever system also checks its own results and adjusts.

3. So what makes it an "information" system?

An information system is an organised combination of people, hardware, software, networks and data that takes in raw facts and turns them into useful information for the business. BrewHaus's till plus the staff using it plus the sales data it stores is a small information system.

4. It is always built from the same five ingredients

Every information system, big or small, is made of five resources: people, hardware, software, data, and networks. And it always does five activities with them: input, processing, output, storage, and control. Once you can spot those, you can understand any system you meet.

5. Data is not the same as information

"450 cups sold" is data: a raw fact. "Saturdays sell double Tuesdays, so staff up" is information: the same fact turned into something useful. Turning data into information is the whole point of the system.

6. There is a family of different system types

Some systems support day-to-day operations (ringing up sales), others support managers making decisions (sales dashboards). Knowing the family tree helps you pick the right tool.

7. These systems have grown more powerful over the decades

They started as simple number-crunchers in the 1950s and grew step by step into today's company-wide, internet-connected systems. Each decade added a new layer of capability.

How the chapter connects: see why systems matter → learn what a system is → add the word "information" → break it into its five ingredients and five activities → see how raw data becomes useful information → meet the family of system types → and watch how they grew over time. After this foundation, every later chapter is just a deeper look at one part of this picture.

Section 1

Why bother with information systems?

You sell coffee, not computers - so why study this at all?

Because for almost any business today, information systems are a make-or-break part of being successful. There are four big reasons they matter:

🏗️

A vital part of any business

They are not optional plumbing; successful companies run on them.

⚙️

They improve efficiency and effectiveness

They make business processes faster, cheaper and better.

🏆

They strengthen your competitive position

In fast-changing markets, good systems help you stay ahead of rivals.

🧭

They support decisions and teamwork

They give managers the information to decide well, and help teams work together.

For BrewHaus, that is the difference between "I think mornings are busy" and "I know exactly which hours, drinks and shops make money, so I staff and stock accordingly."

Section 2

What is a "system" in the first place?

Before "information system," what does the plain word "system" even mean?

A system is a set of interrelated parts, with a clear boundary, working together toward a common goal, by taking inputs and producing outputs through an organised transformation process. That last bit is the heart of it: input → processing → output.

A self-monitoring, self-adjusting system is called a cybernetic system. It adds feedback (data about the system's own performance) and control (watching that feedback and steering the system back toward its goal). Your home thermostat is the classic example: it senses the temperature (feedback) and switches the heating on or off (control).

A business is itself a system. It takes in resources (people, money, materials), runs business processes on them, and outputs goods and services. Management sits in the "control" seat, and information systems provide the feedback that lets managers steer.
Section 3

What makes it an information system?

Add one word - "information" - and what changes?

An information system is any organised combination of:

  • People who use it,
  • Hardware and software that run it,
  • Communication networks that connect it,
  • Data resources it works on, and
  • Policies and procedures that govern it,

that together store, retrieve, transform and share information inside an organisation. Notice it is not just the computer. The people and the rules around it are part of the system too. BrewHaus's loyalty programme is an information system: the app and servers (hardware and software), the internet (network), the customer records (data), the baristas who run it (people), and the rules about points and rewards (procedures).

Section 4

The five ingredients and the five activities

If I open up any information system, what will I always find inside?

Two short lists let you understand any information system. First, the five resources (the ingredients it is built from):

ResourceWhat it meansBrewHaus example
PeopleEnd users and IT specialists.Baristas, the manager, the app developer.
HardwareMachines and media.Tills, tablets, the server, receipt paper.
SoftwarePrograms and procedures.The point-of-sale app, the loyalty app.
DataData and knowledge bases.Sales records, customer preferences.
NetworksCommunication media and support.Wi-Fi and the internet linking the shops.

Second, the five activities every system performs on those resources:

ActivityWhat happens
InputCapture the raw data (a barista rings up a sale).
ProcessingTurn that data into information (add up the day's totals).
OutputPresent the useful result (a sales report).
StorageKeep the data for later (save it in the database).
ControlCheck it is all working correctly (flag a till that does not balance).
Memory hook. Ingredients: People, Hardware, Software, Data, Networks. Activities: Input, Processing, Output, Storage, Control. A business professional should be able to look at any system and name these.
Section 5

Data versus information

Aren't "data" and "information" the same thing?

No, and the difference is the whole reason these systems exist.

DataInformation
Raw facts. Plain measurements about things or transactions, with no context yet. Data that has been turned into a meaningful and useful form: organised, analysed, and placed in context so a person can act on it.
"Cup sold, 8:42am, oat latte, £3.80." "Oat lattes are 40% of morning sales - never run out of oat milk before 9am."

The processing step is what carries you from the left column to the right. Mountains of data are useless until a system turns them into a few clear pieces of information you can use.

Section 6

The family of information systems

Is there just one kind of system, or several?

There is a whole family, and it splits into two main branches by who they help: systems that support operations (the daily work) and systems that support management (decision making).

Operations support systems

  • Transaction processing systems (TPS) - record and process everyday business transactions like sales, inventory and accounting. (BrewHaus's till.)
  • Process control systems - monitor and control physical processes using sensors (for example, sensors watching a refinery, or a roaster controlling bean temperature).
  • Enterprise collaboration systems - help teams communicate and work together, such as email and video conferencing.

Management support systems

  • Management information systems (MIS) - give managers regular, pre-set reports.
  • Decision support systems (DSS) - let managers explore data interactively to answer one-off questions.
  • Executive information systems (EIS) - give top executives the critical, big-picture information they need at a glance.

Other systems

  • Expert systems - give expert-style advice (for example, a credit-application advisor that recommends approve or decline).
  • Knowledge management systems (KMS) - create, organise and share knowledge across the company, such as an intranet of best practices.

Each of these gets its own deeper chapter later (transactions and enterprise systems in Chapter 7, decision support and AI in Chapter 9). Here you just need the map.

Section 7

How information systems grew over time

Why do today's systems do so much more than the ones from decades ago?

Because each era added a new layer of capability on top of the last. Reading from the bottom (oldest) to the top (newest):

EraWhat systems could do
2000s-2010sEnterprise resource planning and business intelligence: company-wide systems, data mining, CRM, supply-chain management.
1990s-2000sElectronic business and commerce over the internet, intranets and extranets.
1980s-1990sStrategic and end-user support: personal productivity, executive information systems, expert systems, systems for competitive advantage.
1970s-1980sDecision support: interactive, on-the-spot help for managers' decisions.
1960s-1970sManagement reporting: pre-set reports to support decision making (MIS).
1950s-1960sData processing: basic transaction processing, record-keeping and accounting.

The trend is clear: from simply crunching numbers, to reporting, to supporting decisions, to creating competitive advantage, to connecting whole companies over the internet.

How a modern business uses all this together. Inside the company, internal networks (intranets) link engineering, manufacturing and accounting. Reaching out, partner networks (extranets) connect suppliers through supply-chain management, and customers through customer relationship management. The internet ties the whole picture together.
Section 8

What the IS function does for a business

Zooming out - what role does the whole information-systems function play?

Pulled together, information systems are:

  • A major functional area of the business, as real as finance or marketing.
  • An important contributor to efficiency, employee productivity, morale and customer service.
  • A major source of information for decision making.
  • A vital ingredient in developing competitive products and services.
  • A dynamic career opportunity.
  • A key part of a modern networked business.

And a small but important lesson from real life: good IT processes matter as much as the hardware and software. A business that backs up its data, plans for failures, and follows sensible procedures gets far more value (and far fewer disasters) than one with shiny tools and sloppy habits.

Section 10

Challenges, ethics and careers

Is technology all upside?

No. Technology brings real risks alongside its benefits, so businesses must manage it responsibly. The chapter frames it as risks and responses:

Potential risks of ITPossible responses
Consumer boycotts, work stoppages, government intervention. Codes of ethics, incentives for good behaviour, professional certification.

On careers, the outlook is mixed but mostly positive: economic downturns and the shift of some jobs to lower-cost regions are real pressures, yet information systems remain a major functional area with strong, growing demand and frequent shortages of skilled people.
