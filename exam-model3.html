Question 1 · Chapter 8

Propose a clicks-and-bricks model for your company

How to answer

Read the warning in the question. She says "no general statements are considered part of the answer." So do not define e-commerce. Go straight to your company's real products and markets.

(1) Say what clicks-and-bricks means in one line, then commit to an integrated model (one brand, shared ERP and inventory) rather than a separate online spin-off, and say why.

(2) Split your offering into a clean two-column table: which products and markets stay bricks (physical, relationship-sold) and which move to clicks (online, low-touch), each with a one-line why.

(3) The logic to use: bricks suit high-value, bulk, custom, contract-and-logistics-heavy, trust-based sales; clicks suit standardised, shippable, broad-audience, low-touch sales plus self-service (ordering, tracking, documents, e-procurement).

Suggested time: about 30 minutes.

Model answer (MIDOR / EPROM)

A clicks-and-bricks model mixes physical operations (the "bricks") with online channels (the "clicks"). For MIDOR / EPROMverify I would adopt an integrated clicks-and-bricks model: one company, one brand, with the online channel running on our existing ERP and inventory rather than a separate online business. We are a B2B refiner, so the physical side must stay dominant, but a focused online layer adds real value.

ChannelProducts & marketsWhy it fits there
Bricks (physical, relationship-sold) Bulk fuels, petrochemical streams and large-volume by-products sold to big industrial buyers, bulk distributors and government tendersverify. High value and high volume, sold on long-term contracts. They need logistics, lab certificates, custom handling, negotiation and trust, none of which is a self-service online sale.
Clicks (online, low-touch) Smaller packaged by-products, lubricants and specialty products for smaller buyers, plus a B2B self-service portal for existing customers and an e-procurement marketplace for suppliersverify. Standardised, shippable and aimed at a broad audience, so they sell well online. The portal also moves low-touch work (order placement, delivery tracking, invoices, certificates, tenders) online, cutting cost and giving us demand data we do not capture today.

Why integrated, not separate: the online channel rides on our existing reputation, customer relationships and ERP, so inventory, pricing and fulfilment stay consistent across both channels and we avoid building a second business from scratch. The bricks close the big deals; the clicks widen reach, speed up the small and repeat transactions, and feed us data, each doing what it is best at.

Question 2 · Chapter 10

Conversion strategies, and the one you would pick

How to answer

(a) Name and define the four conversion strategies from Chapter 10, with the risk and cost trade-off of each. Then answer her "are they relevant to digital transformation?" with a clear yes and a reason: go-live risk still has to be managed, whatever the technology.

(b) Describe a real future project at your company, pick one strategy, and justify it from the project's risk. For a 24/7 refinery, low-risk strategies (pilot or phased) are the natural choice because downtime is dangerous and expensive.

Suggested time: about 30 minutes.

Model answer (MIDOR / EPROM)

(a) The four conversion strategies.

StrategyWhat happensRisk / cost
Parallel Old and new systems run side by side for a while; outputs are compared until the new one is trusted. Safest, but the most expensive and slowest, you operate two systems at once.
Direct (plunge / cut-over) The old system is switched off and the new one switched on at a single point in time. Cheapest and fastest, but the riskiest, if the new system fails there is no fallback.
Pilot The new system goes live in one small unit or site first, then rolls out once it is proven. Low risk, contained blast radius, but slower full rollout.
Phased The new system is introduced module by module over time rather than all at once. Low risk and manageable, but needs old and new to interface during the transition.

Are they relevant to a digital transformation project? Yes. Digital transformation still means switching people from an old way of working to a new system, and that switch-over still carries risk to operations and data. The conversion strategies are exactly the tool for managing that risk and keeping the business running during go-live, so they are as relevant now as they were for traditional MIS projects; you simply pick the strategy that matches the project's risk.

(b) My future project and chosen strategy. A realistic project for us is rolling out a new computerised maintenance and ERP module (or the B2B customer portal) across the refineryverify. I would adopt a phased conversion, with a pilot first: prove it in one unit or one product line, then roll it out module by module. The reason is risk: MIDOR / EPROM runs continuously and a failed big-bang cut-over could disrupt production or safety-critical maintenance, which we cannot accept. A pilot-then-phased approach contains any problem to a small area, lets us train staff and gather feedback before scaling, and keeps the plant running throughout, which matters far more to us than the speed a direct cut-over would give.

Quick recap

Q1What does the clicks-and-bricks question forbid?
General statements. Answer only with your own company's real products and markets, split into bricks (bulk, contract, high-touch) and clicks (standardised, shippable, self-service).
Q2Name the four conversion strategies, riskiest to safest.
Direct (riskiest, cheapest) → Phased → Pilot → Parallel (safest, most expensive).
Q3Which conversion strategy for a refinery project, and why?
Pilot then phased. A 24/7 plant cannot risk a big-bang direct cut-over; contain the risk, train, then scale module by module.
