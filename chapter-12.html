Chapter 12 · The finale

Enterprise & Global Management of IT

Running technology as a managed business function, and stretching it across countries: the CIO's job, outsourcing, IT governance, and the challenges of going global.

Exam radar: the parts that recur are the three components of IT management, the global strategy types (especially transnational), the cultural, political and geo-economic challenges, and transborder data flow. All flagged below.
In plain English - the whole chapter as one story

Our running example: BrewHaus has conquered its home country and wants to expand abroad. This final chapter is about managing technology like a proper business function, and the extra headaches that appear the moment you cross borders.

1. Managing IT has three parts

You manage the strategy (where IT should take the business), the development and technology (building and choosing systems), and the organisation and infrastructure (the IT department and its kit). Three jobs, shared between the top roles.

2. The CIO is a business strategist now

The Chief Information Officer is no longer just "the computer person." They sit at the top table and shape strategy. Technology is too important to leave to technologists alone.

3. You do not have to do it all in-house

BrewHaus can outsource work to specialist firms, and offshore it to lower-cost countries. Cheaper and more focused, but you give up some control.

4. Technology fails when the business ignores it

Most IT failures are not technical, they happen when business managers stay out of IT decisions. Good governance keeps managers involved and accountable.

5. Going global multiplies the challenges

Different cultures, different laws, and the plain difficulty of distance and time zones all complicate running BrewHaus's systems across countries.

6. The end goal is "global yet local"

The most advanced approach, the transnational strategy, runs the whole world as one integrated business on one shared, internet-connected platform, while still fitting each local market.

How the chapter connects: manage IT as three components → led by a strategic CIO → organised sensibly and sometimes outsourced or offshored → kept healthy by governance that involves business managers → then stretched across borders, where cultural, political and geo-economic challenges bite → resolved best by a transnational strategy on one global platform. That closes the loop on the whole course: from what an information system is, to running them worldwide.

Section 1 · exam favourite

Managing IT: the three components

What does "managing IT" actually break down into?

Into three components, each owned by particular leaders. This is a common exam list:

ComponentWhat it coversWho owns it
Managing business and IT strategyWhere technology should take the business.CEO & CIO
Managing application development and technologyBuilding systems and choosing the technology platform.CIO & CTO
Managing the IT organisation and infrastructureThe IT department, its people and its kit.CIO & IT managers

Underpinning all three is the IT architecture: the conceptual design made of the technology platform, the data resources, the application architecture, and the IT organisation.

Section 2

The CIO and the CTO

Who is actually in charge of technology at the top?

Two roles, and the difference matters:

Chief Information Officer (CIO)Chief Technology Officer (CTO)
Oversees all uses of IT and aligns it with strategic business goals. The top, strategy-facing role. In charge of IT planning and deployment, and manages the technology platform. The hands-on, technology-facing role, effectively second in command.
The big shift: the CIO's role has moved from being a technologist to being a business strategist. Tomorrow's CIO sits in company-wide leadership beyond just technology, which is why mentoring successors matters so much given a shortage of qualified IT managers.

Organising the IT function

Should IT be run centrally from head office, or locally by each unit?

The answer has swung back and forth over the decades, and landed in the middle:

Inside the function, managers handle application development (analysis, design, prototyping, programming, project management, quality assurance, maintenance), IS operations (running the data centres, networks and production), and staffing (recruiting, training, rewarding and giving career paths to IT people).

Section 4

Outsourcing and offshoring

Does BrewHaus have to build and run every system with its own staff?

No. Two related options let it hand work to others:

OutsourcingOffshoring
Buying goods or services from a third-party partner that were previously done in-house. Relocating business processes to a lower-cost location, usually overseas (can be production or services).

Companies outsource to cut and control costs, sharpen their focus, reach world-class capabilities, free up internal resources, and share risk. Offshoring grew because so many services went digital and reliable, cheap communication links became available. The trade-off in both cases: lower cost and more focus, but less direct control.

Section 5

Why IT fails, and how governance fixes it

When IT projects go wrong, whose fault is it usually?

IT management can fail in two ways: IT used ineffectively (just computerising old processes instead of inventing better ones) or inefficiently (slow response, downtime, badly run projects). The key insight: the cure is business-manager involvement. IT performs best when managers and end users are actively involved, which is exactly what good governance (steering committees, executive councils) ensures.

A memorable governance idea: there are six big IT decisions that senior management should NOT delegate to the IT department, such as "how much should we spend on IT?", "which processes get the IT money?", and "what security and privacy risks will we accept?" These are business decisions with technical consequences, not technical decisions. Abdicating them is how value gets lost.

A formal framework for all this is COBIT (Control Objectives for Information and Technology), which organises IT governance into four domains: plan and organise, acquire and implement, deliver and support, and monitor.

Section 6 · exam favourite

Going global: the three challenges

What makes running IT across countries so much harder than at home?

Before a global firm can manage any of its IT, it has to overcome three categories of challenge. Memorise these three buckets:

🌐

Cultural

Language, religion and customs, political philosophies, different work styles and business relationships. Staff may need cultural training.

⚖️

Political

Restrictions on moving data across borders, import taxes on hardware and software, local-content laws, rules on spending revenue locally.

📐

Geo-economic

The hard realities of distance and time zones, poor telecoms in places, scarce skilled local workers, and big differences in living and labour costs.

Sitting on top of these challenges, the global firm must then manage five things consistently across every country: its business/IT strategies, its application portfolio, its internet-based platform, its data resources, and its systems development.

Section 7 · exam favourite

The four global strategies

How can a company structure itself across countries, and which is most advanced?

There is a spectrum of four approaches, running from "every country does its own thing" to "the whole world runs as one integrated business":

StrategyHow it operates
MultinationalDecentralised, autonomous units, each country largely doing its own thing.
InternationalHead-office-centric, with operations extended into foreign markets but still controlled from home.
GlobalCentralised and standardised worldwide, with global sourcing and common systems.
TransnationalThe most integrated: the whole world as one cooperative business on one shared, internet-connected platform, global yet local.
The direction of travel is toward transnational. A transnational company treats the world as a single integrated market with mass customisation, global e-commerce, and one common, internet-connected IT backbone, rather than separate country-by-country systems. It blends global integration with local responsiveness. If BrewHaus reaches this stage, a customer in Cairo and one in London get the same seamless service from one global platform, each tuned to local taste.
Section 8 · exam favourite

Transborder data flow

Why can't a global company just move its data freely between countries?

Transborder data flow (TDF) is the movement of business data across national borders over networks. It sounds harmless, but many countries restrict it, and the exam likes the reasons why. A country may see cross-border data flows as violating:

  • Its sovereignty, because the data avoids local customs duties and regulations.
  • Laws protecting its local IT industry from outside competition.
  • Laws protecting local jobs.
  • Its privacy legislation.

This is why, for example, transferring European personal data abroad requires meeting strict privacy conditions (notice, the ability to opt out, access to your data, and adequate security). For a global BrewHaus, this means it cannot simply pool every country's customer data in one place; it has to respect each country's data laws. (This connects straight back to the privacy themes of Chapter 11.)
