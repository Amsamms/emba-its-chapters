Prediction · exam-day playbook

What to expect, and how to win

Pulling all the papers together: the single most likely paper, what to write before you walk in, and how to spend the three hours. Prepare this and you are covered whichever version she sets.

The safest bet

Two papers cover almost everything

Her newest design is the 2026 AI-era paper (AI, ERP, data strategy, emerging tech), and it carries the "no single model answer, be creative" note. That is the direction she is heading, and it is the one your study group marked most important, so it is the most likely next paper. The classic four-question paper (strategy, decision support, e-business + laws, reflection) is the strong backup she has reused for years. Prepare both and you have covered the ground either way, because the same pillars (strategy, ERP, data, e-commerce, the Egyptian laws, decision support, reflection) sit inside both.

The themes that appear no matter the format: a strategy-to-IT table (Chapter 2), enterprise systems / ERP (Chapter 7), data and BI (Chapters 5 & 9), e-commerce (Chapter 8), the two Egyptian laws (Chapter 11), how you decide (Chapter 9), and a reflective closer.
If I had to bet

The predicted paper

Most likely she sets the AI-era structure with her usual personal-and-company hooks inside it:

Predicted · most likely
  1. AI & strategy - how AI supports strategic decisions, three processes in your company that would benefit, and the ethical/legal/operational risks (with the Egyptian laws). Worked answer →
  2. ERP & enterprise integration - strategic importance, implementation challenges, and impact on processes, people and decisions. Worked answer →
  3. Data strategy & BI - data governance and quality, how BI improves performance, and the challenges of becoming data-driven. Worked answer →
  4. Emerging tech & e-commerce - two emerging technologies, preparing for disruption, and how they support e-commerce in your company. Worked answer →

If instead she runs the classic paper, every answer is on the classic paper page. The True/False block, if it appears, is fully answered on the True/False page.

Before you walk in

What to pre-write and memorise

These reappear in every version, so having them ready saves you most of the thinking on the day:

  1. Your company case - two or three sentences on who MIDOR/EPROM is and its strategy (cost leadership through operational excellence). Used in Q1 of every paper.
  2. The strategy-to-IT table - the five strategies (cost leadership, differentiation, innovation, growth, alliance) each with a "use IT to..." line. See it →
  3. Your personal decision steps tied to the rational model (intelligence, design, choice), plus "the tools inform, the human decides". See it →
  4. The two Egyptian laws, cold: Data Protection 151/2020 and Cyber Crime 175/2018, their essence and the "executive regulations came late" critique. See it →
  5. This year's Gartner Top 10 trends - check the current list in case Q1c asks for "Gartner trends for [year]" instead of generic IT.
  6. AI / ERP / data / e-commerce talking points for your company, from the AI paper.
  7. Your reflection answers - two "still curious" questions and one "question I would set". Easy marks. See it →
  8. Chapter 5 facts for any True/False block (data warehouse, data mining vs OLAP, MIS/DSS/KMS, ERP). See it →
What you can safely skip: Chapters 3 (hardware), 4 (software) and 6 (networks). She has never examined them directly.
On the day

The three-hour plan

Four broad questions in three hours means roughly 40 minutes each, with time to spare. A simple rhythm:

TimeDo this
0-10 minRead the whole paper. Decide which version it is. Jot your company case and the strategy table in the margin while they are fresh.
10-150 minAnswer the four questions, about 35 minutes each. Lead with the question you are most ready for. Always draw the table where asked, and keep every answer about your company.
150-180 minDo the reflection question (easy marks), then re-read and add any missing specifics, real names, numbers, your own opinion.
Three habits that earn marks with her: (1) always make it about your real company, never generic; (2) give a table whenever she hints at one; (3) end with your own opinion or critique, she rewards executive-level reflection.
Final check

Walk-in checklist

1Can you write your company + strategy in three sentences from memory?
If not, rehearse it, MIDOR/EPROM, petroleum refining, cost leadership through operational excellence. It opens Q1 of every paper.
2Can you draw the five-strategy "use IT to..." table from memory?
Cost leadership, differentiation, innovation, growth, alliance, each with an IT action. This is the single highest-value thing to memorise.
3Can you name and critique both Egyptian laws?
Data Protection 151/2020 and Cyber Crime 175/2018; critique: modern, welcome, but the executive regulations and enforcement came late.
4Do you have your AI / ERP / data / e-commerce points ready for your company?
Yes if you have read the AI paper, predictive maintenance, process optimisation, ERP integration, BI dashboards, a B2B portal.
5Have you checked this year's Gartner Top 10?
Do it the night before, Q1c sometimes pins the strategy table to the current Gartner trends.
