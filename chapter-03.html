Chapter 3 · Foundation

Computer Hardware

The physical machines: the kinds of computers, the parts inside every one of them, and the devices for getting data in, out, and stored. The "body" that the rest of the course runs on.

Good to know: this is a background chapter. The course exam rarely asks about hardware directly, so read it to be comfortable with the vocabulary, not to memorise specs. The few ideas worth keeping are flagged as you go.
In plain English - the whole chapter as one story

Our running example: BrewHaus, your coffee chain. Chapter 1 said an information system is built from people, hardware, software, data and networks. This chapter zooms in on one of those ingredients: the hardware, the physical machines.

1. Computers come in three sizes

From the small (the laptop on your desk), to the medium (a server in the back room running the loyalty app), to the large (a mainframe a bank uses for millions of transactions). Same idea, different muscle.

2. Every computer has the same handful of parts

No matter the size, a computer takes input, does processing in its brain (the CPU), keeps things in storage, sends out output, and has a control part keeping it all in step. It is the same input-process-output system idea from Chapter 1, in metal and silicon.

3. The brain keeps getting faster

The processing chip has roughly doubled in power every couple of years for decades. That is why the phone in your pocket beats the room-sized computers of the past.

4. Underneath, it is all just on/off switches

Every photo, price and name is stored as tiny on/off signals: bits. Eight bits make a byte, which holds one character. Pile up bytes and you get kilobytes, megabytes, gigabytes.

5, 6. Getting data in and out

Input devices (keyboard, touchscreen, scanner) put data in. Output devices (screens, printers) get it back out. BrewHaus's till is input; its receipt printer and customer display are output.

7. Storage is a trade-off

Fast memory is expensive and small; cheap storage is big but slow. So computers use a mix: speedy memory for right-now work, and cheaper disks and drives for keeping things long-term.

How the chapter connects: computers come in three sizes → but all share the same input-process-store-output-control parts → the processing brain keeps getting faster → everything inside is bits and bytes → we feed data in with input devices and get it back with output devices → and we keep it on storage that trades speed against cost. That is the whole machine.

Section 1

The three sizes of computer

Is a "computer" one thing, or many?

Computers come in a range of sizes, and we group them into tiers by power and price:

TierWhat it isTypical use
MicrocomputersPersonal computers: laptops, desktops, tablets, hand-helds.One person's everyday machine. BrewHaus's office laptop.
Midrange systemsMore powerful network servers, once called minicomputers.Running a website, an intranet, or a shared business app. BrewHaus's loyalty-app server.
MainframesLarge, fast, powerful machines with huge capacity.High-volume transaction processing. A bank's core system.
SupercomputersThe most powerful of all, using parallel processing.Massive scientific and engineering number-crunching.

A small, useful idea here: a terminal is any device that gives you access to a computer, from a "dumb" screen with no brain of its own, to a point-of-sale register, which is a transaction terminal. BrewHaus's till is exactly that.

Section 2

What is inside any computer

Whatever the size, what parts are always in there?

A computer is a system of hardware parts organised by function. There are five functional jobs, and they map exactly onto the input-process-output picture from Chapter 1:

The CPU (central processing unit) is the brain, and it has two halves:

  • The control unit, which reads the instructions and directs the work, and
  • The arithmetic-logic unit (ALU), which does the actual sums and comparisons.

Right next to the CPU is primary storage (main memory), which holds the data and instructions the computer is using right now. Bigger, slower secondary storage (drives) keeps everything for the long term.

Section 3

Speed, and why computers keep getting faster

Why does new kit always seem to leap ahead of last year's?

Computers do their work in almost unimaginably tiny slices of time: today's machines act in nanoseconds (billionths of a second) and faster. The "clock speed" you see advertised, in gigahertz, counts billions of cycles per second. A related idea is throughput: how much useful work the machine gets done in a given time.

Moore's law is the famous observation that the number of transistors packed onto a chip roughly doubles every 18 to 24 months. That steady doubling is why computing power has exploded while prices have fallen, and why the phone in your pocket out-muscles the room-sized computers of decades past.
Section 4

Bits, bytes and how we measure storage

When a computer stores the word "latte," what is it really storing?

At the bottom, everything is just two-state on/off electrical signals. From that simple idea:

  • A bit is a single on/off value, a 0 or a 1. The smallest piece of data.
  • A byte is a group of eight bits, and it holds exactly one character, one letter, digit or symbol. So "latte" is five bytes.

Stack bytes up and you get the storage sizes you already know, each step about a thousand times bigger than the last:

UnitRoughly
Kilobyte (KB)a thousand bytes
Megabyte (MB)a million bytes
Gigabyte (GB)a billion bytes
Terabyte (TB)a trillion bytes
Petabyte (PB)a quadrillion bytes
Section 5

Getting data in: input devices

How does information get into the machine in the first place?

Through input devices. There is a whole range, and they keep getting more natural to use:

  • Keyboard and pointing devices - the mouse, trackball, pointing stick, touchpad.
  • Touchscreens - tap the screen directly (BrewHaus's self-order kiosk).
  • Pen-based input - a stylus on a tablet that turns handwriting into text.
  • Speech recognition - speaking to the computer; the most natural input of all, though it has to learn to understand continuous, conversational speech.
  • Optical scanning and OCR - a scanner reads a paper document, and optical character recognition turns the picture of text into actual editable text. This is how barcodes are read and mail is sorted.
  • Other readers - magnetic stripe cards, smart cards, digital cameras, and the magnetic-ink characters at the bottom of bank cheques (MICR).
Section 6

Getting results out: output devices

Once the computer has done its work, how do we see the result?

Through output devices, mainly screens and printers:

  • Video displays - the old bulky CRT, the flat LCD panel that replaced it, and plasma displays used in large flat screens.
  • Printers - inkjet printers (cheap to buy, great for colour and photos, costlier per page) and laser printers (better for high-volume business printing).
  • Voice response - the computer speaks back, increasingly common alongside screens.
Section 7

Storage, and the trade-off at its heart

Why doesn't a computer just use one big, fast type of storage for everything?

Because of an unavoidable trade-off: the faster a storage type is, the more it costs and the less it holds. So computers layer different types together.

The main storage types, from fastest to cheapest:

  • Semiconductor memory (primary storage). The fastest. Two kinds: RAM, the main working memory you can read and write, but which is volatile (it loses everything when the power goes off); and ROM, permanent read-only memory holding built-in programs (called firmware).
  • Magnetic disk (secondary storage). Hard drives. Fast access, high capacity, reasonable cost. Several disks can be combined into a RAID array so that if one fails, the data survives.
  • Optical disk. CDs and DVDs, good for distributing software and archiving.
  • Magnetic tape. Cheapest, but slow. Used for backups and archives.
  • Flash drives. Small chips that keep data without power, durable and portable.
One more distinction worth keeping: with direct (random) access, like a disk, the machine jumps straight to any item. With sequential access, like tape, it must read through everything in order to reach what it wants. That is why tape is fine for backups but poor for everyday lookups.
Section 8

RFID and grid computing

Two modern ideas worth a quick look. Why do they matter to a business?

RFID (radio-frequency identification) is a way of tagging physical objects with a tiny chip so a reader can identify and track them from a distance, without a line of sight. Compared to a barcode, it can be read further away and can store real data, not just an ID number. Shops use it to track stock, and supply chains use it to follow pallets from factory to shelf. The catch is privacy: things can be read without people realising. If BrewHaus tagged its milk crates, it would know in real time which shop has stock and which is about to run out.

Grid computing pools many ordinary computers over a network so their combined, often idle, power acts like one supercomputer. The appeal is cost: you get huge processing power without buying expensive dedicated hardware. It is how some research and financial tasks get done affordably.
