The 2026 AI-era paper

Her newest design, and the one Eng. Mahmoud flagged as the most important to solve. Four themes: artificial intelligence, ERP and enterprise integration, data strategy and business intelligence, and emerging technologies. It carries an explicit instruction to be creative.

Where it comes from in the course: Q1 draws on Chapter 9 (AI, expert systems, decision support) and Chapter 11 (ethics, the Egyptian laws). Q2 is Chapter 7 (ERP/CRM/SCM) with a little Chapter 10 (implementation). Q3 is Chapter 5 (data resource management) with Chapter 9 (BI). Q4 is the Gartner trends plus Chapter 8 (e-commerce).

The real paper

Important note printed on the paper

The questions below are designed to encourage strategic thinking, practical application, critical analysis, and executive-level reflection. There is no one model answer. Try to be open and creative as much as you can.

Question 1. Artificial Intelligence and Strategic Innovation. "AI is increasingly becoming a strategic business enabler."

  1. Discuss how Artificial Intelligence can support strategic decision-making in organizations.
  2. Identify three business processes within your organization or sector that could benefit from AI adoption. Justify your choices.
  3. Evaluate the ethical, legal and operational risks associated with AI implementation in enterprises. Do you think the Egyptian IT legislations can help with this? Why and how?

Question 2. ERP Systems and Enterprise Integration. "Enterprise systems play a central role in business integration and operational excellence."

  1. Explain the strategic importance of ERP systems in modern organizations.
  2. Discuss the key implementation challenges organizations face during ERP deployment.
  3. Evaluate the impact of ERP systems on organizational processes, employees, and decision-making.

Question 3. Data Strategy and Business Intelligence. "Data has become one of the most valuable organizational assets."

  1. Explain the strategic importance of data governance and data quality management.
  2. How can Business Intelligence (BI) and analytics improve organizational performance and competitiveness?
  3. Discuss the challenges organizations face in becoming data-driven enterprises.

Question 4. Emerging Technologies and Business Disruption. "Emerging technologies, like those reported by Gartner Observatory, continuously reshape industries and markets."

  1. Select two emerging technologies discussed in the course and explain their strategic business impact. How can they support your organizational strategy?
  2. Discuss how organizations can prepare for technology-driven disruption. How can they safely introduce emerging technologies?
  3. How can Gartner emerging technologies strategically support eCommerce introduction or development in your company? Explicitly state the relevant technologies.

Question 1 · Chapters 9 & 11

Artificial Intelligence and strategic innovation

How to answer

(a) AI in strategic decision-making. Tie it to the decision pyramid from Chapter 9: strategic decisions are unstructured. Explain that AI does not replace the manager (her hook elsewhere is "decision making remains human"), it strengthens each step, gathering and cleaning data, spotting patterns a person would miss (data mining), running "what-if" scenarios faster, and giving expert-style advice. Name the AI tools from Chapter 9: expert systems, neural networks that learn from data, machine learning, intelligent agents.

(b) Three processes. Pick three real processes in your sector and justify each with a concrete benefit (cost, speed, safety, quality). One sentence of justification per process is enough.

(c) Risks + Egyptian law. Group the risks into the three she names: ethical (bias, privacy, jobs), legal (liability, data protection), operational (bad data, over-trust, security). Then connect to Chapter 11: yes, the Egyptian laws help, the Data Protection Law 151/2020 governs personal data used to train AI, and the Cyber Crime Law 175/2018 covers misuse and security. Give your own critique (good laws, but the executive regulations came late).

Suggested time: about 25 minutes. Aim for a table in (b).

Model answer (MIDOR / EPROM)

(a) At a refinery, the biggest strategic decisions, how much to invest in a new unit, which product slate to chase, how to hit tightening fuel standards, are exactly the unstructured decisions at the top of the decision pyramid. Artificial intelligence supports these without taking them over. It pulls together data we already drown in (process historians, lab results, maintenance logs, market prices), cleans it, and surfaces patterns no engineer has time to find. Machine-learning models forecast equipment behaviour and product demand; optimisation and "what-if" models test scenarios ("what if we shift the crude blend?") in minutes instead of weeks; and expert systems capture the judgement of our most senior operators so it is not lost when they retire. The final call stays human, but it is made on far better information, faster.

(b) Three processes at MIDORverify that would benefit most:

ProcessHow AI helpsWhy it matters here
Predictive maintenance of rotating equipment (pumps, compressors, turbines) ML on vibration, temperature and pressure sensors predicts a failure before it happens. An unplanned shutdown of a refinery unit costs enormous money per day; catching it early protects both output and safety.
Process optimisation (advanced process control on the distillation and conversion units) AI continuously tunes operating set-points to maximise valuable yield and minimise energy use. Small yield and energy gains on a large throughput turn into large, repeatable savings, our core cost-leadership lever.
Health, Safety & Environment monitoring Computer vision watches for leaks, missing PPE or unsafe acts; models predict emission excursions. Safety and environmental compliance are existential for a refinery; AI gives an always-on extra layer.

(c) The risks fall into the three groups the question names. Ethical: models can be biased or opaque, and staff fear AI will replace them, so we must be transparent and frame AI as a tool that assists operators, not one that fires them. Legal: who is liable if an AI recommendation causes an incident, and any personal data (employees, contractors, customers) used must be handled lawfully. Operational: AI is only as good as its data, bad sensors give confident wrong answers, and people may over-trust it or it may widen the cyber-attack surface. Yes, the Egyptian legislation helps. The Data Protection Law 151/2020 sets the rules for any personal data we feed into AI (consent, purpose, security, the new Data Protection Centre), and the Cyber Crime Law 175/2018 criminalises tampering with or attacking these systems, which protects the plant's digital backbone. My critique: both are solid, modern laws that put Egypt on the map, but their teeth arrived slowly, the executive regulations took years, so enforcement still lags the technology. For a refinery, they give a clear compliance framework to build AI on, which is reassuring for an investment this sensitive.

Question 2 · Chapter 7 (with Chapter 10)

ERP systems and enterprise integration

How to answer

(a) Strategic importance. Define ERP from Chapter 7: one integrated system, one shared database, joining finance, procurement, inventory, maintenance, HR and sales so departments stop working in silos. Strategic value: a single version of the truth, cross-department processes, better decisions, and it underpins "operational excellence", which is exactly the phrase in her hook.

(b) Implementation challenges. List the classic ones: cost and time, data migration and data quality, heavy customisation versus adopting standard processes, integration with existing systems, and above all change management (people resisting new ways of working). You can borrow the Chapter 10 idea of choosing a conversion strategy (parallel, pilot, phased, direct) for the go-live.

(c) Impact. Split into the three the question gives: processes (standardised, integrated), employees (retraining, new roles, some resistance), and decision-making (real-time, shared data, better and faster decisions).

Suggested time: about 25 minutes.

Model answer (MIDOR / EPROM)

(a) An ERP system is a single, integrated suite running on one shared database that ties together finance, procurement, materials and spare-parts inventory, plant maintenance, HR and sales. For a refinery this is strategically vital: our work is deeply cross-functional, a maintenance job needs spare parts (inventory), a purchase order (procurement), a budget (finance) and skilled people (HR) all at once. Without ERP those live in separate systems and nothing reconciles. With it, everyone works from one version of the truth, processes flow across departments, and management gets reliable numbers. That integration is precisely the "operational excellence" the question points to, and at MIDORverify operational excellence and cost control are the heart of our strategy.

(b) The main challenges we would face deploying or upgrading ERP (we run an SAP-style ERPverify):

  • Cost and time: large licence, hardware and consultant costs over a long project.
  • Data migration and quality: years of equipment, materials and vendor records must be cleaned and moved; "garbage in" cripples the new system.
  • Customisation vs standardisation: a refinery has unusual maintenance and inventory needs, but every customisation adds cost and makes future upgrades harder. The discipline is to adopt standard best-practice processes where we can.
  • Integration with plant systems (the control system, the process historian, the lab system) so ERP sees real operational data.
  • Change management: the hardest part. Experienced engineers and operators must give up familiar spreadsheets and trust a new screen. This needs training, communication and visible management backing, and a careful go-live (a phased or pilot conversion from Chapter 10 rather than a risky direct cut-over).

(c) On processes: ERP standardises and connects them, a single integrated flow from purchase request to payment, with full traceability. On employees: roles change, some manual jobs disappear while new analyst and data-entry-discipline roles appear; people need retraining and reassurance, which is why change management matters. On decision-making: managers get real-time, consistent data instead of arguing over whose spreadsheet is right, so decisions on maintenance priorities, spare-part stock levels and spending are faster and better founded. Net effect for us: tighter cost control and higher reliability, our two strategic must-wins.

Question 3 · Chapter 5 (with Chapter 9)

Data strategy and business intelligence

How to answer

(a) Governance & quality. Use Chapter 5: data is a corporate asset, so it must be managed like one. Data governance = the rules, ownership and accountability for data; data quality = accurate, complete, consistent, timely. Why strategic: every decision and every AI/BI tool rests on it, bad data means bad decisions, no matter how clever the analytics.

(b) BI & analytics. Pull from Chapter 9: data warehouse, OLAP, dashboards, data mining. Explain how turning raw data into insight improves performance, spotting trends, monitoring KPIs in real time, finding hidden patterns, and so competing better.

(c) Challenges of becoming data-driven. Data silos, poor quality, skills shortage, culture (people trusting gut over data), cost, and security/privacy.

Suggested time: about 25 minutes.

Model answer (MIDOR / EPROM)

(a) A refinery is one of the most data-rich places there is: thousands of sensors stream readings every second into the process historian, plus lab analyses, maintenance histories and commercial data. That data is only an asset if it is governed and clean. Data governance means clear ownership and rules, who owns the equipment master data, how a tag is named, who may change it, so the same number means the same thing everywhere. Data quality means it is accurate, complete, consistent and timely. This is strategic because every downstream tool, the ERP, the BI dashboards, any AI model, is only as trustworthy as the data underneath. A single mislabelled sensor or a stale price feeds straight into a wrong decision.

(b) Business Intelligence turns that governed data into competitive advantage. We would pull operational and commercial data into a data warehouse, then use OLAP to slice it (yield by unit, by month, by crude type) and dashboards to show managers live KPIs, throughput, energy intensity, on-stream availability, refining margin. Data mining can then find patterns we did not think to look for, for example which combinations of operating conditions quietly raise energy cost. The result is sharper performance: we see problems sooner, benchmark units against each other, and make pricing and planning decisions on evidence rather than instinct, which is exactly how a cost-led refiner stays competitive.

(c) Becoming genuinely data-driven is hard. Silos: the control system, lab system, maintenance system and finance often do not talk, so data is scattered. Quality: sensors drift and records are inconsistent, so trust has to be earned. Skills: data scientists who also understand refining are scarce. Culture: long-serving staff may trust experience over a dashboard, the same "decision making remains human" tension, so analytics must support people, not lecture them. Security and cost round it out. The way through is to start small with one high-value use case, prove it, and build trust from there.

Question 4 · Gartner trends & Chapter 8

Emerging technologies and business disruption

How to answer

(a) Two emerging technologies. Pick two you can tie to your sector and to this year's Gartner list, then state the strategic impact of each. Choose ones you can speak about concretely.

(b) Preparing for disruption + safe adoption. Talk about scanning trends (the "Gartner Observatory" idea), building skills, piloting small before scaling, managing risk and security, and a culture open to change. The Chapter 10 conversion strategies (pilot/phased) are a clean way to say "introduce it safely".

(c) Gartner tech for e-commerce. Bring in Chapter 8. Even a B2B refiner has e-commerce opportunities (a customer or supplier portal). Name specific technologies, cloud, AI personalisation, secure payments, data analytics, and say how each supports going online. Explicitly state the technologies, she asks for that.

Suggested time: about 25 minutes.

Model answer (MIDOR / EPROM)

(a) Two emerging technologies with the biggest strategic impact for us:

  • Industrial AI and the digital twin / IoT: a live digital model of the plant fed by IoT sensors lets us simulate, predict and optimise operations before acting in the real unit. Strategic impact: higher reliability, better yields and safer operations, directly serving our cost and operational-excellence strategy.
  • Agentic / generative AI: AI that can draft procedures, summarise huge technical and maintenance documents, and assist engineers. Strategic impact: it captures scarce expertise and speeds up engineering and decision support across EPROM's services.

(b) To prepare for disruption we have to watch the horizon, treating the Gartner trends as an early-warning radar, and build the skills and data foundation now so we are ready when a technology matures. The safe way to introduce any of it is the Chapter 10 discipline: never a big-bang switch on a live refinery. We pilot on one unit or one process, prove the value and the safety, then roll out in phases, keeping the old way running in parallel until the new one is trusted. Pair that with strong cyber-security and clear governance, and emerging tech becomes an opportunity rather than a threat.

(c) MIDOR is mainly a B2B businessverify, but there is real e-commerce opportunity in a digital portal, for customers buying refined products and by-products, and for suppliers in e-procurement. Gartner-class technologies that would support building it, stated explicitly: cloud platforms (to host the portal cheaply and scale it), AI / machine learning (demand forecasting and personalised offers to customers), data analytics and BI (understanding buying patterns), secure digital payment and identity (safe transactions), and blockchain / smart contracts (tamper-proof contracts and traceable supply for fuels). Together these let a traditional refiner open a modern, secure online channel without disrupting the plant.

Quick recap

Q1In one line, how should you frame AI in a strategy answer for her?
As a tool that strengthens human decision-making (better data, pattern-finding, what-if speed, captured expertise), never as a replacement, "decision making remains human".
Q2What is the single biggest ERP implementation challenge to stress?
Change management, people resisting new ways of working, handled with training, communication, management backing, and a safe phased/pilot go-live (Chapter 10).
Q3Why is data governance "strategic", in one sentence?
Because every decision, ERP, BI and AI tool rests on the data, so without governance and quality even the cleverest analytics produce wrong answers.
Q4What two laws must appear when she asks about AI/IT risk?
Data Protection Law 151/2020 and Cyber Crime Law 175/2018, plus the critique that their executive regulations came late.
