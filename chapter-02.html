Chapter 2 · Exam favourite

Competing with Information Technology

How companies use technology not just to run the business, but to actually beat their rivals. This is the chapter about turning computers into a competitive weapon.

In plain English - the whole chapter as one story

Our running example: imagine you own BrewHaus, a small but growing chain of coffee shops. Across the next twelve chapters we will keep coming back to BrewHaus. In this chapter, the question is simple: how do you use technology to win against the coffee shop across the street?

1. Technology is not just a helper anymore

It used to be that computers just did the boring back-office jobs: payroll, accounting. Today, technology can be the very thing that makes customers choose you. A loyalty app that remembers a regular's usual order is not "support" work. It is the reason they keep coming back. When a system actually helps you win customers, we call it a strategic system.

2. Five forces are always pushing on your business

BrewHaus is never alone. Five pressures squeeze every business: the rival cafe next door, brand-new cafes that might open, substitutes like making coffee at home, demanding customers who want it cheaper, and suppliers (your bean roaster) who want to charge more. Before you can fight, you have to see who you are fighting.

3. There are five basic ways to fight back

You can be the cheapest (cost leadership), be different and special (differentiation), keep inventing new things (innovation), get bigger into new places (growth), or team up with partners (alliance). You can mix several of these at once.

4. Technology powers every one of those five moves

Each strategy has technology behind it. Cheapest? Automate to cut costs. Different? Build an app feature nobody else has. Growing? Use systems to manage shops in new cities. The famous table in this chapter just matches each strategy to the tech moves that make it real.

5. You can also lock the doors behind you

Smart technology makes it painful for customers to leave (all their loyalty points are with you) and hard for new rivals to catch up (you have years of data they do not). That is how IT defends a lead, not just builds one.

6. The whole point is customer value

None of this matters unless customers feel looked after. Modern winners track what each person likes and serve them anytime, anywhere. The tool for this is called CRM - software that remembers everything about each customer so you can treat them as an individual.

7. Look at your business as a chain of value-adding steps

From buying beans to serving the cup, BrewHaus is a chain of activities. Map the chain, then ask: where can technology add the most value? Maybe smart ordering for inbound beans, maybe a CRM at the customer-service end.

8. Sometimes you rebuild a process from scratch

Tweaking is fine, but occasionally you throw out an old way of doing things and redesign it completely with technology. That bold redesign is called reengineering. Big reward, big risk.

9, 10, 11. Be quick, team up, and remember what you know

Markets change fast, so you want to be agile (able to change quickly). You can join forces with partners to form a virtual company that acts like one business. And you want to capture the know-how in your people's heads - knowledge management - so it is not lost when someone leaves.

How the chapter connects: see the five forces pressing on BrewHaus → pick from the five strategies to fight them → power each strategy with technology → defend your lead with lock-in → keep the customer at the center → find the best spots on your value chain → reengineer the processes that need it → stay agile, partner up, and bottle your knowledge. That is the entire chapter.

Section 1

Technology as a weapon, not just a tool

If everyone has computers, how can a computer give you an edge?

For a long time, technology sat in the back room. It processed the payroll and printed invoices. Useful, but invisible to customers. The big shift this chapter describes is that technology moved to the front line. It became a reason customers pick one company over another, and a reason a company can do something its rivals simply cannot.

When an information system is used specifically to help a company compete, we give it a special name.

A strategic information system is any system that uses technology to do one of three things: gain an advantage over competitors, reduce a disadvantage you are suffering, or reach an important business goal. If a system does none of those three, it is just plumbing. If it does any of them, it is strategic.

For BrewHaus, an ordinary till is plumbing. But a system that predicts which shops will run out of oat milk on a rainy Saturday, so you never lose a sale, is strategic. Same technology family, completely different role.

Section 2

The five forces pushing on every business

Before you can compete, who and what are you actually competing against?

A famous way to size up your situation is to look at the five forces that press on any company. They are not all "competitors" in the obvious sense, and that is the useful part: it makes you notice threats you might miss.

Walking through them for BrewHaus:

  • Rivalry of competitors. The other coffee shops already on your street.
  • Threat of new entrants. A new cafe that could open next month.
  • Threat of substitutes. Not another cafe, but a different way to get the same result: a pod machine at home, an energy drink, tea.
  • Bargaining power of customers. If customers can easily push you on price or walk away, they hold power over you.
  • Bargaining power of suppliers. If only one roaster sells the beans you need, that roaster can raise prices and you are stuck.
Section 3

The five basic ways to fight back

Once you see the forces, what moves can you actually make against them?

There are five basic competitive strategies. This is the most important list in the chapter, so it is worth memorising. A handy way to remember them: Cool Dogs In Green Apartments (Cost, Differentiation, Innovation, Growth, Alliance).

💰

Cost leadership

Become the lowest-cost producer, or help your customers and suppliers lower their costs. Win on price.

✨

Differentiation

Make your product clearly different and better in a way customers care about, so price is not the only thing that matters.

💡

Innovation

Find brand-new products, services, or ways of working that did not exist before.

📈

Growth

Expand into new regions or new product lines, getting bigger and reaching more customers.

🤝

Alliance

Team up with other companies, suppliers, or even competitors to do together what you could not do alone.

An important subtlety. These five are not mutually exclusive. A single move can belong to more than one. And watch the trap: not everything innovative makes you different from rivals, and not everything that makes you different is truly innovative. They overlap, but they are not the same idea.
Section 4

How technology powers each strategy

Fine, five strategies - but where does the technology actually come in?

This is the table the exam loves. It takes each strategy and lists the concrete ways technology makes it happen. Read it as "to pull off this strategy, use technology to do these things."

StrategyUse technology to…
Lower costs Cut the cost of your own business processes; and help lower the costs of your customers or suppliers too.
Differentiate Add new tech features that set your product apart; blunt a rival's advantage; and aim your product at a specific niche of customers.
Innovate Create new products that have technology built in; open up brand-new markets; and make radical changes to processes that slash cost, lift quality, or speed up time to market.
Promote growth Manage expansion across regions and countries; and branch out into new products and services.
Develop alliances Build virtual partnerships; and link your systems to customers, suppliers and subcontractors over the internet so you work as one team.

For BrewHaus: lower costs with automated stock ordering, differentiate with an app that saves your favourite drink, innovate with pay-by-face checkout, grow with systems that run shops in new cities, and form an alliance by plugging your ordering straight into your bean roaster's system.

Section 5

Other ways technology defends your position

Once you are ahead, how do you stop rivals from catching up?

Beyond the five basic strategies, there are extra moves that use technology to defend a lead rather than build one:

  • Lock in customers and suppliers. Make your relationship so convenient and integrated that leaving is a hassle.
  • Create switching costs. Give customers something they would lose by leaving - like years of saved loyalty rewards or saved preferences.
  • Raise barriers to entry. Build something so big or so data-rich that a newcomer cannot easily copy it.
  • Build strategic technology capabilities. Get so good at using technology that the skill itself becomes an advantage.
  • Leverage your technology investment. Squeeze extra value out of systems you have already paid for.

BrewHaus's loyalty app does two of these at once: the saved rewards are a switching cost, and the years of order history are a barrier a new cafe cannot match on day one.

Section 6

Putting the customer at the center

All this strategy talk - what is the actual goal?

The goal is customer value. A striking point in this chapter: for most customers today, quality, not price, is the main thing that decides value. Companies that win do a few things consistently:

  • Track each person's individual preferences.
  • Keep up with market trends.
  • Supply products, services and information anytime, anywhere.
  • Tailor service to the individual customer.
  • Use a CRM system to keep the focus on the customer.
CRM (Customer Relationship Management) is software that remembers everything about each customer - what they buy, when, what they like, every past interaction - so the whole company can treat them as a known individual rather than a stranger. The internet, internal networks (intranets) and partner networks (extranets) let that customer picture be shared with employees and partners everywhere.

This is why BrewHaus's app greets a regular by name and suggests their usual order. The app is just CRM wearing a friendly face.

Section 7

The value chain: where should tech go first?

You cannot upgrade everything at once - so where does technology add the most value?

The value chain is a way of seeing your company as a chain of activities, each adding a bit of value on the way to the finished product. Splitting those activities helps you spot where a smart system would pay off most. The activities come in two kinds:

TypeWhat it meansBrewHaus examples + a tech system for each
Primary processes Steps that directly make or deliver the product to the customer. Getting beans in (smart just-in-time stock system); making drinks (efficient kitchen displays); selling (point-of-sale ordering); after-sale care (CRM).
Support processes Steps that keep the company running and help the product indirectly. HR (staff benefits intranet); technology development (a recipe-sharing portal with partners); buying supplies (supplier web portals).

The lesson: map your chain, then aim technology at the links where it creates the biggest jump in value, all pointing toward one goal - competitive advantage.

Section 8

Reengineering: rebuild it, do not just tweak it

When is improving a process not enough?

Most of the time you improve things gradually. But sometimes a process is so broken that small fixes are pointless, and you redesign it from a blank sheet. That bold redesign has a name: Business Process Reengineering (BPR) - the fundamental rethink and radical redesign of a process to get dramatic improvements in cost, quality, speed and service. The payoff is large, but so is the risk of disruption and failure. Technology is the main thing that makes it possible.

Here is the difference between gentle improvement and full reengineering, side by side:

 Business improvementBusiness process reengineering
Level of changeSmall, step by stepRadical
Starting pointThe process you already haveA clean, blank slate
Time requiredShortLong
ScopeNarrow, inside one departmentBroad, across many departments
Who drives itBottom-up (from staff)Top-down (from leaders)
Main enablerQuality controlInformation technology
RiskModerateHigh
A way to picture it: business improvement is renovating one room of your house. Reengineering is demolishing the house and building a better one on the same plot. Bigger result, far more that can go wrong.

A classic example is order management. Taking an order really flows across many departments - proposal, commitment, configuring the product, credit check, delivery, billing, collecting payment. No single department owns the whole thing, which is exactly why it is a great candidate to reengineer with cross-department systems like ERP, CRM and supplier-linked inventory.

Section 9

Becoming an agile company

The market keeps changing fast - how do you keep up?

The old market sold standardised products to a mass audience, slowly, with little information. The new market is global, fragmented into niches, individualised, fast-moving and information-rich. To thrive in it you need agility: the ability to prosper in rapidly changing markets by selling high-quality, customer-configured products, using internet technologies.

Technology supports three flavours of agility:

Type of agilityThe ideaReal example
Customer agility Bring customers in as a source of ideas and as testers of new products. eBay's users effectively act as its product-development team, posting thousands of suggestions a week.
Partnering agility Tap the knowledge and assets of suppliers and partners. Yahoo! grew from a search engine into a portal through many content partnerships.
Operational agility Achieve speed, accuracy and low cost in everyday operations. Ingram Micro let customers and suppliers connect straight into its ordering systems.

For BrewHaus, customer agility could mean letting app users vote on the next seasonal drink, turning your customers into your test kitchen.

Section 10

The virtual company

What if you could borrow other companies' strengths instead of building your own?

A virtual company uses technology to link together separate organisations, assets, people and ideas so they act like a single business, even though they are not one company. They build inter-enterprise systems that connect customers, suppliers, subcontractors, and sometimes even competitors. Inside the firm, intranets join the teams; out to partners, extranets join the alliances.

Why bother? Companies form virtual companies to:

  • Share information and risk with partners.
  • Combine each partner's core strengths.
  • Cut the time from idea to cash by sharing work.
  • Reach more facilities and markets.
  • Get into new markets and share customer loyalty.
  • Shift from selling products to selling complete solutions.

If BrewHaus partners with a local bakery, a delivery app and a bean roaster, all connected through shared systems, customers experience "BrewHaus" as one smooth service even though four companies are involved.

Section 11

Knowledge management: bottling what your people know

When your best barista quits, does their know-how walk out the door with them?

A knowledge-creating company keeps making new business knowledge, spreads it through the whole company, and builds it into its products and services. To do that, it helps to know there are two very different kinds of knowledge:

Explicit knowledgeTacit knowledge
Things written down or stored in computers: data, documents, manuals. The "how-to" living in people's heads: skill, judgement, experience. Hard to write down, and often the most valuable knowledge a company has.

The central job of knowledge management is to turn that hidden tacit know-how into something shared. In practice it means creating the techniques, technology, systems and rewards that get employees to share what they know, so the company makes better use of its accumulated knowledge.

Knowledge management systems (KMS) are a major strategic use of technology. They help knowledge workers create, organise and find important know-how, and make it available wherever and whenever it is needed. The kinds of content they hold include processes, procedures, patents, best practices, formulas, forecasts and fixes.

For BrewHaus, that means your star barista records their latte-art tricks and bean-pairing notes into a shared system, so every new hire in every city learns from the best - even after that barista has moved on.

Watch out

Easy to confuse

Thisversus that
Competitive forces - the five pressures pushing on you (rivals, entrants, substitutes, customers, suppliers). Competitive strategies - the five moves you make in response (cost, differentiation, innovation, growth, alliance).
Differentiation - being clearly different from rivals. Innovation - being clearly new. Something can be different without being new, and new without being different.
Business improvement - small, gradual, low risk, one department. Reengineering (BPR) - radical, clean-slate, high risk, across departments.
Explicit knowledge - written down, stored in computers. Tacit knowledge - in people's heads, hard to capture.
Primary processes - directly make or deliver the product. Support processes - keep the firm running, help indirectly.
Intranet - private network inside the company. Extranet - secure network reaching out to partners and suppliers.

Q1Name the five basic competitive strategies.
Cost leadership, differentiation, innovation, growth, and alliance. Remember they are not mutually exclusive - companies often combine several.
Q2What three things makes an information system "strategic"?
It uses technology to gain a competitive advantage, reduce a competitive disadvantage, or meet an important strategic business goal.
Q3List the five competitive forces.
Rivalry among existing competitors, threat of new entrants, threat of substitutes, bargaining power of customers, and bargaining power of suppliers.
Q4How would you "use IT to" support a cost-leadership strategy?
Use technology to substantially cut the cost of your own business processes, and to help lower the costs of your customers and suppliers.
Q5What is the difference between business improvement and reengineering?
Improvement is incremental, short, narrow and lower risk. Reengineering (BPR) is a radical, clean-slate, cross-department, technology-enabled redesign with high reward and high risk.
Q6Explain explicit versus tacit knowledge, and what KM tries to do.
Explicit knowledge is written down or stored in computers; tacit knowledge is the know-how in people's heads. Knowledge management uses systems and rewards to get people to share knowledge, especially turning tacit know-how into something the whole company can use.
Q7What is CRM and why does it matter for competing?
Customer Relationship Management software remembers everything about each customer so the company can treat them as a known individual. It supports the modern truth that quality of the relationship, not just price, drives customer value.
