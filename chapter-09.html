Chapter 9 · Exam favourite

Decision Support Systems

The systems that help people decide: from simple reports, to interactive "what-if" models, to executive dashboards, all the way to artificial intelligence and expert systems.

Exam radar: heavily examined, and three contrasts come up again and again: MIS vs DSS, OLAP vs data mining, and expert systems vs data mining. Each is spelled out below. Also know the three decision levels.
In plain English — the whole chapter as one story

Our running example: BrewHaus managers make decisions all day, from "reorder milk?" to "which new city next?" This chapter is about the systems that help them decide well, and how those systems climb from simple to genuinely intelligent.

1. Decisions come in three difficulty levels

Some are routine and rule-based (reorder when stock hits 10) — those are structured. Some are wide-open judgement calls (should we expand abroad?) — unstructured. Many sit in between. Higher up the company, the more open-ended the decisions get.

2. A report is only useful if the information is good

Right time, right content, right form. A perfect figure that arrives a week late, or buried in noise, is worthless.

3. MIS gives you the standard reports

The original decision-support tool just produces regular, pre-set reports for routine decisions: today's sales, this week's stock. Reliable, but rigid.

4. DSS lets you play with the numbers

For trickier decisions you want to ask "what if?" interactively. A DSS lets a manager model scenarios: "what if we cut prices 10%?" Flexible, exploratory, manager-driven.

5. OLAP shows you the data; mining finds the surprises

OLAP lets you slice sales by city, month and product to answer questions you have. Data mining digs through history to find patterns you did not know to ask about (oat-latte buyers love almond croissants).

6. At the top end, the computer starts to think

Executive dashboards summarise everything for the boss. And artificial intelligence, especially expert systems, lets a computer give expert-style advice by following rules captured from human experts.

How the chapter connects: decisions range from structured to unstructured → good information makes them possible → MIS delivers routine reports → DSS adds interactive what-if modelling → OLAP and data mining dig into the data (viewing vs discovering) → executive systems and portals summarise it for leaders → and AI, led by expert systems, lets the machine itself advise. The tools climb from reporting to genuine reasoning.

Section 1 · exam favourite

The three levels of decision making

Are all business decisions the same kind of problem?

No. They sort into three levels, which line up with three layers of management. The key idea: the higher up you go, the less structured the decisions and the more summarised and forward-looking the information needs to be.

Decision typeLevelMeaning
StructuredOperationalThe steps to decide can be specified in advance (reorder when stock hits 10).
Semi-structuredTacticalSome steps can be pre-set, but not enough to reach the right answer automatically.
UnstructuredStrategicThe steps cannot be specified in advance (should we enter a new country?).
Section 2

What makes information good

Why is one report useful and another useless, even with the same facts?

Because the quality of information has three dimensions, and a report can fail on any of them:

⏰

Time

Timely, current, frequent enough, covering the right period.

📦

Content

Accurate, relevant, complete, concise, the right scope.

🎨

Form

Clear, right level of detail, well ordered, well presented.

A spot-on sales figure that reaches a BrewHaus manager a week late (bad time) or buried in a hundred-page dump (bad form) has lost almost all its value.

Section 3

MIS: the standard reports

What was the original tool for supporting managers?

The Management Information System (MIS) is the original decision-support tool. It produces regular reports, displays and responses to meet day-to-day needs, serving operational and tactical managers who face structured decisions. Its reports come in four flavours:

Report typeWhen it appears
Periodic scheduledA fixed format on a regular schedule (the daily sales report).
ExceptionOnly when something unusual happens (a shop misses target).
DemandOn request, whenever the manager asks for it.
PushSent to the manager automatically, without being asked.
Section 4 · exam favourite

DSS: playing with the numbers

What if a manager needs to explore, not just read a fixed report?

That is what a Decision Support System (DSS) is for. It supports semi-structured decisions through an interactive modelling process: the manager uses analytical models, special databases and their own judgement to explore options on the fly. Crucially, DSS users are not asking for a pre-set report — they are testing scenarios. Here is how it differs from MIS, the exam's favourite comparison:

 MISDSS
What it givesInformation about how the organisation is performing.Tools and techniques to analyse a specific problem.
Form & frequencyPeriodic, scheduled, pre-set reports.Interactive inquiries and responses.
FormatFixed, pre-specified.Ad-hoc, flexible, adaptable.
How it worksExtracts and manipulates business data.Analytical modelling of business data.

The heart of a DSS is its model base, and there are four classic kinds of analysis a manager runs with it:

  • What-if analysis — change a variable and see what happens ("what if we cut advertising 10%?").
  • Sensitivity analysis — a special what-if: change one variable repeatedly to see how sensitive the result is to it.
  • Goal-seeking analysis — the reverse: set a target and let the system find the input needed ("what sales hit £1m profit?").
  • Optimisation analysis — go further: find the best value given constraints (maximise profit within a budget).
Section 5 · exam favourite

OLAP versus data mining

Both dig into data, so how are they different?

OLAP (online analytical processing) lets you examine data interactively from many angles, in real time. It has three signature operations:

  • Consolidation (roll-up) — aggregate up to a higher level (shop sales rolled up to city level).
  • Drill-down — the reverse: go into the underlying detail (city totals broken down per product).
  • Slice and dice — view the data from different angles (the same sales by time, by region, by product).

Data mining is different. It provides decision support through knowledge discovery: it digs through vast historical data to find patterns, trends and correlations you did not know were there. A common use is market basket analysis, finding which products are bought together (used for cross-selling and product placement).

The exam contrast, cleanly. OLAP is user-driven: you already have the question and you slice the data to answer it ("show me sales by city by month"). Data mining is discovery-driven: it finds the surprises you did not think to ask about. OLAP shows you the data; mining finds the hidden gold in it.
Section 6

Executive systems and portals

What does the boss at the top of BrewHaus use?

An Executive Information System (EIS), which combines features of MIS and DSS to give top executives immediate, easy access to the information critical to their strategic goals. Its hallmark features are tailored dashboards, exception reports, trend analysis, and drill-down (start at the big summary, click into the detail). EIS proved so useful it spread beyond executives to managers and analysts.

Pull it all together and you get an enterprise information portal (also called an enterprise knowledge portal): a single web front door that integrates MIS, DSS, EIS, OLAP and data-mining tools, personalised per user, often with a digital dashboard. Two specialised cousins worth a mention: geographic information systems (GIS) that put data on maps for location decisions, and data visualisation systems that let you explore data as interactive 3-D graphics.

Section 7

Artificial intelligence

What happens when we want the computer itself to act intelligently?

That is artificial intelligence (AI): a multidisciplinary field (drawing on computer science, maths, biology, linguistics, psychology and engineering) aiming to make computers think, see, hear, talk and reason like people. AI splits into three application areas:

AI domainWhat it covers
Cognitive scienceThe "thinking" branch: expert systems, neural networks, fuzzy logic, genetic algorithms, intelligent agents, learning systems.
RoboticsThe "doing" branch: vision, touch, dexterity, locomotion, navigation.
Natural interfacesThe "communicating" branch: natural language, speech recognition, virtual reality.

For business decisions, the most important member of the cognitive-science branch is the expert system.

Expert systems

How can a computer give expert advice?

An expert system is a knowledge-based system that holds the knowledge of a specific, complex area and acts as an expert consultant to its users. It is built from four components:

The knowledge is usually stored as IF-THEN rules captured from human experts by a knowledge engineer. The easy way to build one is an expert system shell: a ready-made system with the inference engine and interface already built, into which you pour your own knowledge base.

BenefitsLimitations
Captures and preserves expert knowledge; faster and more consistent than a person; never tired; can hold several experts' knowledge. Narrow focus; cannot learn on its own; costly to build and maintain; only works in a small, well-defined domain.
Expert system versus data mining (a classic exam question). An expert system reasons forward from knowledge a human expert put in (rules), to give advice; it does not discover anything new and cannot learn. Data mining works the opposite way: it discovers new patterns out of raw data; knowledge is the output, not the input. One applies known expertise; the other finds unknown patterns.
Section 9

Neural networks, fuzzy logic and friends

What other kinds of AI are there, and how do they differ from expert systems?

  • Neural networks — computing modelled on the brain's web of neurons. Unlike an expert system, a neural network learns patterns directly from data. (This "can it learn?" point is the key contrast: expert system = no, neural network = yes.)
  • Fuzzy logic — reasoning with vague, imprecise information using terms like "very high" instead of exact numbers, much closer to how humans actually reason. Used in controllers for trains, elevators and cars.
  • Genetic algorithms — "survival of the fittest" software that evolves better and better solutions, useful when thousands of possible answers exist.
  • Intelligent agents — software "bots" that act on your behalf, using built-in and learned knowledge to carry out tasks (filtering email, searching, navigating).
  • Virtual reality — computer-simulated, multi-sensory 3-D environments (also called telepresence), used in design, training, medicine and simulation.

Q1Name the three decision-structure types and the management level of each.
Structured (operational), semi-structured (tactical), and unstructured (strategic). Higher up the company, the less structured the decisions.
Q2Give the main differences between MIS and DSS.
MIS gives fixed, periodic, pre-specified reports about organisational performance; DSS gives interactive, ad-hoc, flexible analytical modelling to explore a specific problem.
Q3List the four kinds of DSS analytical modelling.
What-if, sensitivity, goal-seeking, and optimisation. (Sensitivity is a special what-if; goal-seeking is the reverse of what-if; optimisation extends goal-seeking.)
Q4What are the three OLAP operations, and how does OLAP differ from data mining?
Consolidation (roll-up), drill-down, and slice-and-dice. OLAP is user-driven exploration of data you understand; data mining is discovery-driven, finding hidden patterns you did not know to ask about.
Q5What are the four components of an expert system?
A knowledge base, an inference engine, a user interface, and a knowledge-acquisition facility.
Q6How do an expert system and data mining differ?
An expert system applies knowledge a human expert put in (rules in, advice out) and cannot learn; data mining discovers new patterns from raw data (data in, knowledge out).
Q7What makes a neural network different from an expert system?
A neural network learns patterns directly from data; an expert system only applies pre-loaded rules and cannot learn on its own.
