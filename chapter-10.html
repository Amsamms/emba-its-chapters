Chapter 10 · Exam favourite

Developing Business/IT Solutions

How a new system actually gets built and rolled out: the step-by-step life cycle, deciding whether to build or buy, and the four ways to switch everyone over to the new system.

Exam radar: heavily examined, and one topic is almost guaranteed: the four system conversion strategies (parallel, direct, pilot, phased) and which to pick. Also know the five SDLC stages and the five feasibility types. All flagged below.
In plain English — the whole chapter as one story

Our running example: BrewHaus decides to replace its ageing ordering system with a new one. This chapter is the project plan: how you go from "we need a new system" to "everyone is happily using it," without disaster.

1. Treat the problem like a system

Before building anything, you diagnose the situation properly: define the problem, weigh options, pick one, design it, build it, then check it worked. Methodical, not a guess.

2. There is a standard five-step life cycle

Investigate → analyse → design → implement → maintain. Each step produces something concrete before you move on. Skipping steps is how projects fail.

3. First ask: is this even worth doing?

A feasibility study checks whether the project makes sense, on cost, on technology, on people, on the law. Better to find out now than halfway through.

4. Work out WHAT before HOW

Analysis pins down what the system must do (the requirements). Only then does design decide how to do it. Mixing these up causes chaos.

5. You do not always build from scratch

BrewHaus can build the system, buy a ready-made one, or rent it. And whatever it acquires, it evaluates against clear factors before committing.

6. Switching everyone over is the dangerous moment

The riskiest step is going live. There are four ways to do it, from a clean overnight switch to running old and new side by side, each with its own risk and cost. Choosing wisely is the whole game.

How the chapter connects: diagnose the problem as a system → follow the five-stage life cycle → check feasibility before committing → define WHAT is needed, then design HOW → build or buy it against clear evaluation factors → and finally go live using one of the four conversion strategies, then maintain it. From idea to live system, safely.

Section 1

The systems approach

Where do you even start when a business has a problem to solve?

You apply the systems approach, a structured problem-solving method. Applied to building software, it is called information systems development. It runs in seven steps: recognise the problem, develop a feasible solution idea, develop and evaluate alternatives, pick the best one, design it, implement it, then evaluate whether it worked.

Systems thinking means "seeing the forest and the trees." Instead of blaming one cause for poor sales, you look at the whole situation as a system, its input, processing, output, feedback and control, and find where it is actually breaking. Maybe BrewHaus's slow sales are not "bad staff" but out-of-date procedures (processing) fed by wrong information (feedback).
Section 2 · exam favourite

The systems development life cycle

What are the actual stages of building a system?

The systems development life cycle (SDLC) has five stages, and each one produces a concrete deliverable before you move on. This pairing of stage and product is exam gold:

Section 3 · exam favourite

Is the project feasible?

Before spending a penny, how do you know the project is worth doing?

You run a feasibility study: a quick check of users' needs, costs, benefits and whether the project can realistically be done. It tests five kinds of feasibility:

FeasibilityThe question it asks
OperationalWill it actually solve the problem and fit how the business works?
EconomicDo the benefits beat the costs (cost/benefit analysis)?
TechnicalCan the hardware, software and network do it, in time?
Human factorsWill employees, customers and management accept and use it?
Legal / politicalAny legal, contractual or power-structure problems?
Section 4

Analysis: nail down WHAT, not HOW

What is the single most important rule of the analysis stage?

Separate what the system must do from how it will do it. Analysis produces the functional requirements: a clear statement of what each business activity needs the system to do, with no decisions yet about the technology. A logical model is the blueprint that shows what the system does without saying how. Getting this wrong, jumping to "how" too early, is a classic cause of failed projects.

Logical vs physical. Logical = what (the requirements, the blueprint). Physical = how (the actual hardware, software and code). Analysis is logical; design and implementation make it physical.
Section 5

Design and prototyping

Once you know what is needed, how do you design it?

Design turns requirements into system specifications, across three areas: the user interface (screens, forms, reports), the data (how it is structured), and the processes (the programs and procedures).

A powerful technique here is prototyping: quickly building a rough working model, showing it to users, and refining it again and again until they are happy. It makes development faster and easier, especially when nobody can describe exactly what they want up front, and it pulls end users right into the design. BrewHaus's staff try a mock-up of the new till screen and say "move that button," and the next version reflects it.

Section 6

Build, buy, and how to evaluate

Does BrewHaus have to build the whole thing itself?

No. It can build it, buy a ready-made package, or use a service. Whatever it acquires, it evaluates vendors properly: write a request for proposal (RFP/RFQ) stating the minimum requirements, send it to vendors, and judge the bids, demanding demonstrations, checking other users' experiences, and benchmarking (running test data to compare performance). Hardware is judged on a list of ten factors (performance, cost, reliability, compatibility, technology, ergonomics, connectivity, scalability, software, support); software adds quality, efficiency, flexibility, security, maintenance and documentation.

The headline rule for software: "software that is slow, hard to use, bug-filled or poorly documented is not a good choice at any price." A cheap system that staff hate is not a bargain.
Section 7

Implementation

The system is built. What does it take to actually put it into use?

Implementation has four keys: testing (debug everything before going live), data conversion (move the old data across, cleaned and corrected, this is a major cause of failure if done badly), documentation (user guides and system records), and training (without it, even a great system fails). And then the big one: converting everyone over to the new system.

Section 8 · the exam star

The four conversion strategies

This is the exam question. How do you switch from the old system to the new one?

There are four ways to make the switch, and each trades risk against cost and speed. This is the most examined idea in the chapter, so learn all four with their pros and cons:

StrategyHow it worksRiskCostBest when…
Direct (plunge / cold turkey)Switch off the old, switch on the new, in one go.HighestLowestEmergencies, or when old and new cannot coexist. No safety net.
ParallelRun old and new together until you trust the new one.LowestHighest (up to 4x)Replacing a manual system; when failure would be very costly.
PilotLaunch the new system at one representative site first, then expand.ContainedModerateCompanies with many locations.
PhasedBring the new system in one component at a time.ModerateModerateLarge systems; combines the strengths of direct and parallel, but takes the longest.
If the exam asks which you would adopt (for a digital-transformation project, say), give a reason, not just a name. A safe answer: a phased or pilot approach for a big, risky rollout, because it limits the damage if something goes wrong, with parallel reserved for the most critical systems you cannot afford to lose, and direct only when there is no choice.
Section 9

Maintenance and managing the change

Once it is live, is the job done?

Not at all. Maintenance is the single most costly activity over a system's life, fixing errors, improving performance, and adapting to change. It comes in four types: preventive, corrective, adaptive and perfective.

And throughout, the hardest part is often the people. Systems fail not because the technology is bad but because users resist. The cure is change management: involve users early, train them well, communicate constantly, secure top-management commitment, and work with the company culture rather than against it. A brilliant system nobody will use is a failed system.

Test yourself

Quick self-check

Try to answer each in your head, then click to reveal.

Q1Name the five SDLC stages and their products.
Investigation → feasibility study; analysis → functional requirements; design → system specifications; implementation → operational system; maintenance → improved system.
Q2What are the five types of feasibility?
Operational, economic, technical, human factors, and legal/political.
Q3Name and describe the four conversion strategies.
Direct (switch over in one go, riskiest, cheapest); parallel (run both together, safest, most expensive); pilot (one representative site first, then expand); phased (introduce the new system one component at a time, slowest but lower risk).
Q4Which conversion strategy is safest, and why is it costly?
Parallel, because the old system keeps running as a live backup. It is costly because you operate both systems at once, up to four times the cost of the old system alone.
Q5What is the difference between logical and physical models?
A logical model shows what the system does (the requirements); a physical model shows how it does it (the actual technology). Analysis is logical; design and build are physical.
Q6Why do systems projects fail even when the technology is fine?
User resistance. The fix is change management: involve users early, train them, communicate, secure management commitment, and work with the company culture.
